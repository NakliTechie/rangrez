Preface

China has the world's oldest continuous named-colour vocabulary. Specific colour terms appear in the Shijing (the Book of Songs, compiled between the eleventh and the seventh centuries before the common era) — the earliest surviving Chinese poetry anthology — and by the Han dynasty (206 BCE – 220 CE) those terms had been organised into a full cosmological system under the doctrine of the Five Elements (wuxing), which paired each of wood, fire, earth, metal, and water with a colour, a direction, a season, an emotion, and an organ of the body. The five colours of the system — green, red, yellow, white, and black — are not descriptive but structural: they are the axes of a working metaphysics that ordered imperial ritual, medical practice, architectural orientation, and personal dress for two thousand years. No other civilisation has maintained a codified chromatic system at this scale for this long.

Three overlapping traditions grow on top of that deep foundation, and the ten palettes in this chapter belong to all three at once. The first is imperial codification: a tradition in which specific colours — above all the yellow of the Forbidden City roof tiles and the red of the seal paste in every formal document — were legally restricted to specific classes of person and specific ritual contexts. The second is the literati tradition: a scholarly, deliberately reserved, predominantly monochrome practice in which the ink-wash painting of Song and Yuan intellectuals became the standard against which a thousand years of Chinese taste measured itself. The third is the popular and festive tradition: the bright vermillion lanterns of the Spring Festival, the red envelopes (hongbao) passed between family members at the lunar new year, the wedding dress of the bride, the firecracker paper on the courtyard floor on the morning of the first day of the year.

The global story grows out of the second and third of these and runs outwards through porcelain. From the early fourteenth century, the kilns of Jingdezhen in Jiangxi Province produced a specific blue-and-white ware (qinghua) whose cobalt pigment was initially imported from Persia — the same lapis-derived material that the Persia chapter follows upstream to the mines of Badakhshan — and whose finished form travelled outward through the Islamic trade network to Ottoman Istanbul, Safavid Isfahan, Mughal Delhi, Portuguese Lisbon, Dutch Amsterdam, and Spanish Mexico, reshaping the chromatic expectations of every culture it reached. By the late seventeenth century, the English word for fine ceramic had become the same as the English word for the country where it was made — china — and no other colour in any other tradition has ever achieved the same linguistic completeness. The ten palettes below track that arc from the Shang bronzes through the Song celadon through the Ming blue-and-white to the Qing export trade, and along the way take in the paper, the ink, the yellow, the cinnabar, the festival red, and the lacquer that make up the rest of the Chinese chromatic foundation.

A Chinese chronology

  • c. 7000 BCEHemudu culture in coastal Zhejiang produces the earliest surviving Chinese lacquered objects — a red lacquered wooden bowl found in 1978 at the Hemudu site remains one of the oldest worked-lacquer objects anywhere in the world.
  • c. 1600 BCEThe Shang dynasty begins. Ritual bronze vessels cast in elaborate zoomorphic forms enter the archaeological record, together with the earliest surviving Chinese writing — the oracle bones of Anyang.
  • c. 11th–7th c. BCEThe poems of the Shijing (Book of Songs) are composed. Specific colour terms — green, red, yellow, white, black — appear throughout. This is the earliest substantial Chinese-language chromatic vocabulary in the written record.
  • c. 4th c. BCEThe Five Elements (wuxing) cosmology takes shape during the Warring States period. Each element is paired with a colour, a direction, a season, and an organ of the body. The system will underwrite Chinese ritual, medicine, and architecture for two millennia.
  • 221 BCEQin Shi Huang unifies China under the Qin dynasty. The Terracotta Army — originally painted in vivid lacquer-based pigments — is buried at his mausoleum at Lintong.
  • 206 BCEThe Han dynasty begins. Lacquer-making reaches its first classical peak; the Mawangdui tombs at Changsha (sealed 168 BCE) will later yield several hundred exceptional painted lacquer objects.
  • c. 105 CECai Lun, a court official, refines and standardises the process of papermaking. Paper begins to displace silk and bamboo as the substrate of Chinese writing.
  • 618 CEThe Tang dynasty begins. Sancai (three-colour) lead-glazed ceramics are produced at Tang-period kilns; the first systematic Chinese tea culture takes shape in Lu Yu's Chajing (Classic of Tea, c. 760).
  • 960 CEThe Song dynasty begins. Longquan celadon, Ru ware, Ding ware, and Jun ware all reach classical form in the Northern and Southern Song periods. Literati ink-wash painting (wenrenhua) is theorised and practised by Mi Fu, Su Shi, and their circles.
  • 1271 CEKublai Khan founds the Yuan dynasty. Within a generation, the Jingdezhen kilns begin to produce blue-and-white porcelain using cobalt ore imported from Persia — the ware known to later Chinese collectors as qinghua.
  • 1368 CEThe Ming dynasty begins. Blue-and-white porcelain becomes the signature Ming export and reaches its technical peak during the Xuande reign (1426–1435). The emperor's official kiln at Jingdezhen is the largest ceramic manufacturing operation in the world.
  • 1405–1433The seven voyages of Zheng He carry Chinese porcelain, silk, and other trade goods as far as the east coast of Africa. Ma Huan, a Muslim Chinese interpreter on the voyages, records the ports they visit in Ying-yai Sheng-lan (The Overall Survey of the Ocean's Shores, 1433).
  • 1557 CEThe Portuguese establish a permanent trading post at Macau. Chinese porcelain begins to reach Europe in quantity through the Lisbon trade.
  • 1602 CEThe Dutch East India Company (VOC) captures the Portuguese carrack San Jago; the porcelain cargo is auctioned in Amsterdam and kickstarts a European fashion for Chinese blue-and-white that will last two centuries.
  • 1644 CEThe Ming dynasty collapses and the Qing dynasty is founded by the Manchu. Jingdezhen is disrupted by the transition but resumes imperial production under the Kangxi (1661–1722), Yongzheng (1722–1735), and Qianlong (1735–1796) emperors.
  • 1712 & 1722The French Jesuit missionary Père François Xavier d'Entrecolles, stationed near Jingdezhen, sends two detailed letters back to Paris describing the full Chinese porcelain manufacturing process. The letters introduce the words kaolin and petuntse into European languages and provide the technical intelligence that will enable European attempts at hard-paste porcelain at Meissen and elsewhere.
  • 1911 CEThe last Qing emperor abdicates. Two and a half thousand years of dynastic rule ends.
  • 1966–1976The Cultural Revolution disrupts every traditional craft in China. Kilns are closed, workshops are dispersed, traditional practitioners are persecuted. The recovery begins only in the 1980s.
  • 2009 CETraditional handicrafts of Xuan paper are inscribed on the UNESCO Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity. In the same year the traditional firing technology of Longquan celadon is inscribed as well.
2.1

白瓷Baizi · White Porcelain§

Chinese baizi: white porcelain. The role slot for LIGHT.

The light role of the collection is the specific warm ivory-white of Chinese porcelain at its finest — not the cold blue-white of modern industrial ceramic, but the faintly creamy, faintly yellowish, slightly translucent white of Dehua blanc de Chine and Song-period Ding ware. Chinese porcelain is the reason the English language uses the word china as a common noun for fine ceramic, and the baizi tradition is the single most important white surface in the material history of Eurasia.

Material

True porcelain requires two ingredients that were, for most of history, available together only in China: kaolin, a specific white clay rich in aluminium silicate, and petuntse, a pulverised feldspathic stone. When the two are mixed, formed, glazed with a lime-alkali mixture, and fired at temperatures above 1,280°C, they produce a dense, vitrified, white, translucent ceramic body that cannot be matched by earthenware, stoneware, or any of the soft-paste imitations that European potters developed during the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries in their unsuccessful attempts to reverse-engineer the Chinese product. The Chinese had the raw materials; they had the kiln technology to reach the necessary firing temperatures from the late Tang period onwards; and they had two millennia of accumulated technical knowledge about how to control the chemistry of clay, glaze, and fire. European potters had none of these things until the early eighteenth century, which is why the ceramic trade ran in only one direction for five hundred years.

Two specific centres produced the canonical Chinese whites. Ding ware, made in Quyang County in northern Hebei from the late Tang through the Song period (roughly the ninth through twelfth centuries), was the favoured white porcelain of the Northern Song imperial court. The characteristic Ding-ware body is an ivory-cream white with a barely perceptible yellow cast, and the better pieces are decorated with incised or moulded designs that are visible only when the light catches the surface at the right angle. Dehua ware (德化瓷), produced in the hills of Dehua County in Fujian Province from the Song period onwards but reaching its mature form in the late Ming and early Qing, is the finer white — so fine, in fact, that the best Dehua pieces are translucent to the point of being usable as lampshades. European collectors called Dehua blanc de Chine, "white of China," and the term stuck.

History

Dehua reached its technical peak in the late Ming and early Qing under the potters He Chaozong, Lin Chaojing, and their contemporaries, who specialised in small figurative sculpture — particularly standing and seated figures of Guanyin, the Bodhisattva of Compassion, whose flowing robes translate perfectly into the slightly fluid Dehua body. The finest surviving Guanyin figures by He Chaozong are in the Shanghai Museum, the British Museum, and the Percival David Foundation collection at the British Museum (now merged into the British Museum's main collections). A single signed piece by He Chaozong remains one of the most valuable categories of Chinese ceramic in the international art market.

The export trade carried Dehua figurines to Europe from the early seventeenth century onwards through the Dutch East India Company. By the late seventeenth century, standing Guanyin figures were being adapted by European potters as the basis for Meissen figurines, and the Dehua export model shaped the entire European figurative ceramic tradition for a century.

2.2

米紙Mizhi · Xuan Paper§

Chinese mizhi: mulberry-fibre scholar's paper. The role slot for WARM.

The warm anchor of the collection is the soft cream of hand-made Chinese paper — specifically Xuan paper (宣纸), the slightly absorbent, slightly translucent mulberry-and-rice-straw paper that has been the substrate of Chinese calligraphy, literati painting, and classical book printing for more than a thousand years. Xuan paper is not white but warm off-white, and the specific ivory-cream tone is part of what gives traditional Chinese ink painting its characteristic depth.

Material

Paper was refined in China in the first centuries of the common era — the court official Cai Lun is traditionally credited with standardising the process around 105 CE, though earlier forms predate him. The basic recipe uses plant fibres (originally hemp, later mulberry, bamboo, and rice straw) that are pulped, cooked with lime, beaten to break the cellulose strands, suspended in water, lifted onto a bamboo mat to drain, pressed, and dried. The specific properties of a given paper — absorbency, flexibility, colour, ageing resistance — depend on which plant fibres are used and in what proportions.

Xuan paper (xuanzhi), made in Jing County in southern Anhui Province since at least the Tang dynasty, is the canonical Chinese calligraphy and painting paper. The traditional recipe uses a mixture of blue sandalwood bark (qingtan) and rice straw, pulped in mountain stream water and processed over months rather than days. The finished paper is unusually strong — good Xuan paper can survive in archival storage for more than a thousand years — unusually absorbent for ink, and carries a specific warm ivory tone that is part of its scholarly identity. The craft of Xuan papermaking was inscribed on the UNESCO Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in 2009.

The Four Treasures of the Study

In the traditional Chinese literary imagination, paper is one of the Four Treasures of the Study (wenfang sibao): paper, ink, brush, and inkstone. The four are treated as a single integrated kit, and serious collectors acquire them together. A Qing-dynasty scholar would own specific named papers from specific workshops, specific ink sticks from Huizhou in Anhui, specific brushes from Huzhou in Zhejiang, and specific inkstones from Duanxi in Guangdong or Shexian in Anhui. Each of the four had its own connoisseurship literature and its own ranking of producers. The best workshops in each category have in many cases survived to the present day, and the current holders of the traditions are treated as living national references.

Xuan paper's particular place in this system comes from its behaviour with ink. Because the paper is absorbent but slow, a loaded brush will produce a controlled spread into the fibres rather than a hard edge, and an experienced painter can manipulate the exact degree of spread by adjusting water content, brush pressure, and stroke speed. The entire Chinese literati painting tradition — see §2.3 SHUIMO — depends on this specific relationship between paper and ink, and it is why a Chinese scholar painter's bag of tricks cannot be transferred to any other surface without loss.

2.3

水墨Shuimo · Ink Wash§

Chinese shuimo: water-ink, ink-and-water. The role slot for MONO.

The monochrome role of the collection is not a colour but a practice — the tradition of literati ink-wash painting and calligraphy in which a single carbon-black ink, diluted across a range of tones from almost dry to watery wash, carries an entire thousand-year aesthetic discipline. Shuimo is the ground of Chinese scholarly art, and its practitioners — Mi Fu, Ni Zan, Dong Qichang, Bada Shanren, Shitao — are among the most consequential figures in the global history of painting.

Material & practice

Chinese ink is made from carbon soot — historically pine soot (songyanmo) or tung-oil soot (youyanmo) — bound with animal glue, shaped into a stick, and dried for weeks or months before being ground on an inkstone with a little water. The better the ink stick, the finer and more regular the particle size of the pigment, and the wider the tonal range a painter can extract from it. A Qing-dynasty ink stick from the Hu Kaiwen workshop in Huizhou (She County, Anhui) is still considered among the finest materials available to any working calligrapher today. The workshop, founded in 1765, has operated continuously since.

The technical range of Chinese ink-wash painting comes from the interaction of three variables: the amount of water in the brush, the pressure of the stroke, and the absorbency of the paper (see §2.2 MIZHI). A well-loaded brush can carry five distinct tones across a single stroke — dark at the tip, progressively paler toward the base — and a skilled painter uses this gradient as an active compositional element rather than an accident. The ink-wash landscapes of the Southern Song master Mi Fu and his son Mi Youren (eleventh–twelfth centuries) are early demonstrations; the Yuan master Ni Zan (1301–1374) pushed the discipline to the extreme, producing spare, dry, almost-empty landscapes in which entire compositions rest on the precise placement of a few strokes against vast areas of untouched paper.

The literati and the court

The central ideological distinction in Chinese painting from the Song period onwards is between the literati tradition (wenrenhua, "scholar painting") and court painting. Literati painting was amateur in the strict sense — it was done by educated gentlemen who held official positions or had retired from them, who wrote poetry and practised calligraphy, and for whom painting was a private act of self-cultivation rather than a professional occupation. The theorist Su Shi (1037–1101) is the founding figure of this tradition; the Ming-period collector and critic Dong Qichang (1555–1636) is its most influential later codifier. Dong's division of Chinese painting into a "Northern School" (professional, colourful, descriptive) and a "Southern School" (literati, monochrome, expressive) was polemical and historically inaccurate in many respects, but it organised Chinese art criticism for three hundred years and still shapes how the tradition is taught today.

The two most extraordinary literati painters of the Ming-Qing transition were both Ming-loyalist monks who went into religious retreat after the Manchu conquest of 1644. Bada Shanren (1626–1705), born Zhu Da, was a descendant of the Ming imperial house who shaved his head and took Buddhist vows at the age of twenty; his spare, cryptic ink paintings of fish, birds, and withered plants carry an encoded political grief that was legible to his contemporaries and that has kept his work at the centre of Chinese critical attention ever since. Shitao (1642–1707), similarly a Ming-loyalist monk, wrote the Huayulu (Remarks on Painting), a short theoretical treatise that remains one of the foundational texts of Chinese aesthetics and one of the most difficult to translate. Both painters are held in major collections worldwide; both continue to serve as touchstones of the shuimo tradition.

2.4

桃花Taohua · Peach Blossom§

Chinese taohua: peach blossom, the specific pale pink of early spring.

Taohua is the Chinese pale pink — the colour of the peach orchard at first light, the emblem of spring and of an idealised rural utopia that has haunted Chinese literary imagination since the fifth century of the common era. It is related to but distinct from the Japanese sakura (§1.4 of the Japan chapter): where the Japanese cherry carries the specific emotional weight of mono no aware (the pathos of impermanence), the Chinese peach carries the weight of Peach Blossom Spring, the pastoral allegory of a hidden valley outside historical time.

The colour

The peach (Prunus persica) is native to northwest China, where it has been cultivated for at least four thousand years, and was moving westward along the trade routes long before the Silk Road took its classical form — the Latin name reflects the ancient European belief that the tree was native to Persia, which was in fact only an intermediate stop. The flower is a specific pale pink, slightly cooler than the Damask rose of Persia (§10.4), slightly warmer than the Japanese cherry, and opens in late March or early April across the central and southern Chinese plains. The trees flower before they leaf, so a peach orchard in bloom is a distinct, almost abstract field of pink against the bare branches and the still-brown ground of early spring.

The Peach Blossom Spring

The literary centrality of the peach in China comes from a single short prose piece by the poet Tao Yuanming (365–427 CE), written in the early fifth century under its title Taohuayuan Ji (Record of the Peach Blossom Spring). The piece is a brief allegory: a fisherman, following a stream upstream, passes through a grove of peach trees in full bloom and emerges into a hidden valley whose inhabitants — descendants of refugees from the wars of three centuries earlier — have lived in peaceful ignorance of the outside world for generations. The fisherman stays for a few days, is treated with hospitality, and is asked not to speak of the valley when he returns home. He agrees, leaves, but cannot resist telling the local magistrate — and when the magistrate sends men to investigate, the valley cannot be found again.

The piece is roughly four hundred Chinese characters long. It has been the most influential short text in Chinese literature for fifteen hundred years. Every subsequent Chinese invocation of pastoral retreat, of utopia, of the hidden valley outside historical time, of the golden age that cannot be returned to, refers to it directly or indirectly. Painters from the Song dynasty onwards produced Taohua yuan scrolls as a recognised subgenre; the theme still appears in contemporary Chinese cinema and literature. The taohua pink is not merely a descriptive colour but a literary signal: the colour of the grove at the entrance to the hidden valley.

The Queen Mother of the West and the peaches of immortality

A second, older layer of meaning attaches to the peach through the cult of Xiwangmu, the Queen Mother of the West, a Daoist goddess whose celestial garden produces peaches of immortality that ripen once every three thousand years. The motif appears in classical texts from the Han dynasty onwards and is a staple of Daoist iconography; a Ming-period court painting of the Queen Mother's peach banquet, showing her distributing the immortality peaches to assembled deities, is a standard image in any museum collection of Chinese art. The peach's association with long life and auspiciousness runs through everything from wedding symbolism to birthday cakes (the shoutao, "longevity peach," is the traditional Chinese birthday cake, shaped and coloured to resemble the fruit).

2.5

黄Huang · Imperial Yellow§

Chinese huang: yellow, specifically the bright warm yellow of Ming and Qing imperial ritual.

Huang is a specific warm, saturated, slightly orange-shifted yellow — the colour of the roof tiles of the Forbidden City, of the ceremonial robes of the Ming and Qing emperors, and of the silk wrappings on imperial seals and documents. For roughly five hundred years, between the early Ming and the fall of the Qing in 1911, it was the single most legally restricted colour in the world: its use outside imperial context could carry the death penalty. The restriction is the reason yellow in the Chinese tradition carries an intensity of meaning that no equivalent colour in any other tradition can match.

The wuxing logic

The imperial weight of yellow grows out of the Five Elements cosmology (wuxing) that took shape during the Warring States period and was fully codified in the Han dynasty. In the wuxing system, each of the five elements — wood, fire, earth, metal, water — is paired with a colour, a direction, a season, and a set of other correspondences. Earth, the central element, corresponds to yellow, to the centre (rather than one of the four compass directions), and to the emperor himself. The emperor was the "Son of Heaven" (Tianzi), the intermediary between the human realm and the cosmic order, and as the central figure in the central realm he was associated with the central colour.

The wuxing association is older than the political restriction. The earliest imperial use of yellow as a specific ritual colour appears to date from the Han or earlier, and references to imperial yellow robes run through Tang and Song sources. The full legal codification, however, is a Ming innovation. From the founding of the Ming dynasty in 1368, specific shades of yellow — particularly the bright minghuang (bright yellow) used on the emperor's official robes and the roof tiles of the palace complex — were reserved by sumptuary law for exclusive imperial use. The Qing dynasty (1644–1911) inherited and intensified the system.

The Forbidden City

The single largest visible expression of imperial yellow in the world is the roof of the Forbidden City (Zijincheng, "Purple Forbidden City") in Beijing, which has roughly 980 surviving buildings and whose principal halls are roofed with glazed yellow tiles of a specific composition developed by the imperial kilns. Seen from any elevated vantage point in modern Beijing, the Forbidden City reads as a sea of bright yellow rectangles against the grey of the surrounding city — a chromatic statement that remains legible at satellite resolution and that is, at its scale, the single clearest architectural expression of a colour restriction anywhere on Earth.

The Forbidden City was built between 1406 and 1420 under the Yongle emperor, the third Ming emperor, and served as the imperial residence of every subsequent Ming and Qing emperor until the abdication of the last Qing emperor, Puyi, in 1911. It was inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage list in 1987, together with the related Imperial Palace of the Shenyang.

青瓷Qingci · Celadon§

Chinese qingci: celadon, specifically the grey-green glazed stoneware of the Song kilns. (The English word celadon comes from a French seventeenth-century pastoral character who wore ribbons of this colour.)

Qingci is the grey-green glaze of Song-dynasty porcelain — specifically the soft, translucent, slightly misty green of Longquan and Yaozhou ware, and the rarer jade-green of Ru ware, which was reserved for the Northern Song imperial court and of which fewer than a hundred intact pieces survive. The specific colour is the result of an iron-oxide glaze fired in a reducing atmosphere, and the twentieth-century potter Bernard Leach described it as "the colour of sky after rain seen through a jade screen."

Material

Celadon is produced by a glaze chemistry that exploits the dual behaviour of iron oxide in a hot kiln. Iron oxide at normal oxygen levels produces a yellow or brown colour; the same oxide fired in a low-oxygen (reducing) atmosphere reduces to ferrous oxide and produces a green-to-blue-grey tone instead. Song-period Chinese potters had mastered kiln atmosphere control to the point where they could produce reliable greens across large batches, which no European tradition matched until the late nineteenth century. The specific chromatic range of a celadon glaze depends on the iron content (typically 1–3% by weight), the firing temperature (around 1,250–1,300°C), the precise duration of the reduction phase, and the thickness of the glaze itself — thicker glaze pools in incised decoration produce deeper green tones, which is why so many Song celadons carry incised or moulded surface patterns.

Longquan and the other kiln traditions

Four major kiln traditions produced celadon during the Song and later periods. Longquan ware (龙泉窑), made in the hills of Longquan County in Zhejiang from the Song period through the Ming, is the most abundant and the most influential — Longquan celadons travelled on every trade route out of China from the twelfth century onwards, reaching Southeast Asia, the Islamic world, East Africa, and eventually Europe. The traditional firing technology of Longquan celadon was inscribed on the UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2009. Yaozhou ware, made in Shaanxi Province, produced a slightly darker, more olive-toned green and is known for deeply carved surface decoration. Ru ware (汝窑), made for roughly twenty years in the early twelfth century at a single set of kilns in Henan Province, was produced almost exclusively for the Northern Song imperial court and carries the most celebrated of all celadon glazes — a pale misty blue-green described in the classical literature as yuguo tianqing, "the colour of the sky after rain." Fewer than a hundred intact pieces of Ru ware survive worldwide, making it one of the rarest categories of historical ceramic, and authenticated Ru-ware pieces have fetched record prices at auction over the past fifteen years. Guan ware, made at the Southern Song imperial kilns in Hangzhou after the court moved south in 1127, was the post-Ru imperial celadon and is similarly rare.

The Korean Goryeo celadon tradition (twelfth–thirteenth century) is a direct offshoot of the Chinese Song tradition — Korean potters learned the reduction-firing technique from Chinese sources and then developed it in a distinctive inlay (sanggam) direction that became a Korean signature. The tradition in Japan is less direct but equally indebted; Japanese tea masters from the Muromachi period onwards prized Chinese celadons as a specific category within the vocabulary of tea utensils.

2.7

青花Qinghua · Blue-and-White§

Chinese qinghua: "blue flower," blue-and-white porcelain. The keystone of the chapter.

Qinghua is the keystone of the China chapter and the direct chromatic descendant of the Lajvard palette in the Persia chapter (§10.7). The cobalt-blue-under-clear-glaze decoration of Yuan and Ming porcelain, developed at the Jingdezhen kilns in Jiangxi Province from the early fourteenth century onwards using cobalt pigment initially imported from Persia, is the single most globally consequential product in the history of ceramic — a ware so desirable that it reshaped the economy of four continents, transformed the interior decoration of Europe for two centuries, and gave the English language the common noun china for the entire category of fine ceramic.

Material & technique

Blue-and-white porcelain is made by painting a design in cobalt oxide pigment directly onto the unfired white porcelain body, covering the painted decoration with a transparent feldspathic glaze, and firing the whole assembly in a single high-temperature firing at around 1,280–1,320°C. The cobalt oxide, under the clear glaze and at high temperature, fuses into the body and produces a deep, slightly violet-inflected blue that cannot be achieved by any other pigment-glaze combination. The colour is permanent, heat-resistant, acid-resistant, and will not fade for centuries. A Yuan-dynasty blue-and-white bowl excavated from a fourteenth-century shipwreck will, after cleaning, look almost exactly as it looked when it left the kiln.

The technical difficulty of blue-and-white is not in the firing but in the painting. Cobalt oxide is black before firing and reveals its true colour only when the glaze has melted and the oxide has fused — which means the painter is working blind, applying one shade of black to a white surface and trusting that it will come out of the kiln as the intended blue. A Jingdezhen painter in the Xuande reign (1426–35) would have been trained for a decade or more before being trusted with imperial work, and the finest Xuande-period bowls carry brushwork of a precision that has rarely been matched anywhere in the history of ceramic decoration.

The Persian cobalt

The cobalt that made Chinese blue-and-white possible in its earliest and finest phases did not come from China. The native Chinese cobalt ores are lower-grade and produce a grey-blue tone rather than the saturated violet-blue that characterises the best Yuan and early Ming pieces. The finer pigment — known in Chinese sources as huihui qing, "Muslim blue" or "Mohammedan blue" — was imported from Persia along the same trade networks that brought Persian carpets, glassware, and manuscript illumination to the Mongol Yuan court. The ultimate source of this cobalt has been debated by specialists, but the current scholarly consensus is that the pigment originated in the Kashan region of central Iran, where cobalt ores were being mined and processed for Persian underglaze ceramic before the Yuan period and from which it could reach the Mongol capitals through Muslim merchants.

This is the direct connection to the Persia chapter. The lapis lazuli of Badakhshan (§10.7 LAJVARD) is a different mineral — lapis is a sodium-calcium aluminosilicate, cobalt is a transition metal — but both pigments travelled the same trade routes, both were refined in Persian workshops, and both produced the signature blue of their respective traditions. The Persian cobalt industry and the Persian lapis trade operated in parallel, and Persia's position as the chromatic crossroads of the pre-modern world is reinforced by the fact that two of the three greatest blue surfaces in the history of art — Renaissance ultramarine and Ming blue-and-white — were both made possible, at least initially, by materials refined on the Iranian plateau.

By the mid-fifteenth century the Chinese had developed domestic cobalt sources to acceptable quality, and the imported pigment became scarce after the end of Zheng He's voyages in 1433. But the visual template had been set during the period of Persian imports, and the later Chinese blue-and-white kept the same aesthetic vocabulary even as the chemistry shifted.

Jingdezhen and the scale of production

The Jingdezhen imperial kilns in Jiangxi Province are the single largest ceramic manufacturing operation in the pre-industrial history of the world. At the peak of Ming production in the sixteenth century, the town had several hundred kilns operating simultaneously, employing tens of thousands of workers across the full production chain — clay processors, potters, painters, glaze specialists, kiln loaders, firemen, quality inspectors, packers, and freight handlers. A single imperial order from the Ming court might run to tens of thousands of pieces; the surviving records show individual orders for fifty thousand, a hundred thousand, or more, delivered over a single year. No other ceramic tradition in the pre-modern world operated at this scale.

The Jingdezhen system was organised into specialised workshops. A single finished piece might pass through seventy separate hands between the preparation of the clay and the final packing — one workshop for throwing, another for trimming, another for underglaze decoration, another for glaze application, another for firing, another for overglaze enamels, another for quality inspection. The division of labour was so complete that a painter might spend an entire career specialising in a single motif (dragons, say, or peonies) and never touch another part of the process. Père d'Entrecolles, the Jesuit missionary who documented the Jingdezhen operation in his 1712 and 1722 letters, described the complete assembly line in detail and noted that his European correspondents would find the scale difficult to believe.

The d'Entrecolles letters

Père François Xavier d'Entrecolles (1664–1741), a French Jesuit who served as superior of the Jesuit mission in Jiangxi Province, sent two detailed letters back to Paris in 1712 and 1722 describing the full Chinese porcelain manufacturing process at Jingdezhen. The letters were published in the Lettres édifiantes et curieuses, the Jesuit mission journal that was widely read in European intellectual circles, and they represent the first systematic European scientific description of Chinese porcelain. D'Entrecolles identified the two principal raw materials — the white clay he transliterated as kao-lin (from the Chinese gaoling, the name of the hill from which the Jingdezhen potters sourced their clay) and the pulverised feldspathic stone he transliterated as pe-tun-tse (from baidunzi, "little white bricks"). Both transliterations entered European languages directly from his letters: kaolin remains the standard technical term in English, French, and German for the specific white clay that makes porcelain possible, and petuntse, while now obsolete, was the standard European term for the feldspathic component throughout the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries.

D'Entrecolles's letters enabled European potters, after decades of failed experimentation with soft-paste substitutes, to identify the specific raw materials they needed. The breakthrough came at Meissen in Saxony, where Johann Friedrich Böttger succeeded in producing the first European hard-paste porcelain in 1708–1710 — a date that is usually cited as just preceding d'Entrecolles's first letter, but the technical knowledge circulating through Jesuit and merchant networks in the decades before his formal publication was already shaping European efforts. The subsequent diffusion of porcelain technology across Europe — Sèvres in France, Capodimonte in Naples, Wedgwood's Jasperware in England — all built on the knowledge base that d'Entrecolles's letters had made public.

Porcelain is made from two substances called kaolin and pe-tun-tse, which are found in the mountains near King-te-chen. The three great export trajectories

Blue-and-white porcelain leaves China along three distinct trade routes, each of which reshapes the destination in specific ways.

The first line runs westward into the Islamic world. From the mid-fourteenth century onwards, Yuan and early Ming blue-and-white reaches Ottoman Turkey, Safavid Persia, and Mamluk Egypt as diplomatic gifts, trade goods, and war booty. The Ottoman sultans begin systematic accumulation of Chinese porcelain from the reign of Selim I (1512–1520) and the practice continues under his successors until the collection at Topkapı Palace in Istanbul reaches approximately ten thousand pieces — the single largest historical collection of Chinese porcelain outside China, and a reference archive that has been essential to every twentieth-century attempt to date and attribute Yuan and early Ming wares. In Iran, the Safavid Shah Abbas I (1588–1629) donated his personal collection of Chinese porcelain to the shrine of his ancestor Sheikh Safi al-Din at Ardabil in 1611, where they were inventoried, stored in a specially built porcelain room, and preserved largely intact; the Ardabil collection, now split between the Iran Bastan Museum in Tehran and the British Museum, is the second essential reference archive for early Chinese blue-and-white.

The impact on Islamic ceramic production was direct and immediate. The Iznik kilns in Ottoman Turkey, from the late fifteenth century onwards, produced their own blue-and-white tile and ceramic in explicit imitation of Chinese originals — often copying specific Chinese decorative motifs before gradually developing their own Ottoman vocabulary. The same process occurred in Safavid Isfahan, where Persian potters produced blue-and-white ceramic that straddled the line between imitation and original tradition. Much of what European observers have subsequently called "Islamic tile" — from the Iznik panels of the Rüstem Pasha Mosque in Istanbul to the cobalt domes of the Imam Mosque in Isfahan — is chromatically descended from Chinese blue-and-white by way of Persian intermediaries.

The second line runs south into Southeast Asia and down to maritime Southeast Asia. From the fifteenth century onwards, huge quantities of Chinese blue-and-white reach Vietnam, Siam, the Malay peninsula, Java, and the Philippines, where they are used in royal courts, village shrines, dowry exchanges, and funerary practice. Shipwreck archaeology in the South China Sea and the Java Sea has recovered tens of thousands of Chinese blue-and-white pieces from dozens of sunk Ming-period trading vessels, and the distribution pattern shows that the export volume south was at least comparable to, and probably greater than, the volume west. The pieces themselves are often of lower technical grade than the imperial work that reached Topkapı, but they constitute the bulk of Chinese ceramic actually produced in the period.

The third line runs west again, but by sea this time, into Europe. The maritime route opens when the Portuguese reach China via the Cape of Good Hope in the early sixteenth century and establish their trading post at Macau in 1557. The Dutch capture the Portuguese carrack San Jago in 1602 and auction the Chinese porcelain cargo in Amsterdam, touching off a European fashion that will last for two centuries and that the Dutch will dominate through the VOC monopoly. The characteristic Dutch response is Delftware — tin-glazed earthenware decorated in blue on white in explicit imitation of Chinese originals — which in turn shapes the ceramic traditions of England (Bristol and Lambeth), France (Rouen and Nevers), Portugal (the blue-and-white azulejo tradition of seventeenth- and eighteenth-century Lisbon), and Mexico (the Talavera poblana of Puebla, which is documented in the Mexico chapter of the Bibliothèque at §8.7). All of these traditions are second-generation imitations of Chinese blue-and-white, most of them routed through a Persian or Ottoman intermediary before the Dutch got to them. The visual genealogy is: Persian cobalt → Jingdezhen kilns → Iznik and Isfahan tile → Delft → Bristol, Lambeth, Rouen, Lisbon, Puebla.

The sum is that a single Chinese ware, made at a single Chinese town, using (initially) a Persian-imported pigment, reshaped the ceramic traditions of at least a dozen major cultures across five centuries and gave the English language a common noun for the entire category. No other material object in the global history of art has had a comparable reach, and no other colour in the global history of material culture has travelled as far on its own merits.

2.8

朱砂Zhusha · Cinnabar§

Chinese zhusha: cinnabar, mercury sulfide, the red pigment of imperial seals.

Zhusha is the deep, slightly orange-saturated red of mercury sulfide — the pigment that has, for more than two thousand years, filled the red seal paste (yinni) at the bottom of every Chinese calligraphy, painting, official document, and personal letter. Every time a Chinese artist, scholar, or official finished a piece of work and pressed their seal into the soft cinnabar paste before applying it to the paper, they were leaving a mark made of the same material that has been mined in China since the Han dynasty — and the specific red of zhusha carries the full weight of Chinese ritual and official authority.

Material

Cinnabar is the mineral form of mercury sulfide (HgS), a heavy crystalline substance with a characteristic deep red-orange colour. When ground to a fine powder, it produces the specific pigment known in Western art history as vermilion — a name that, via Latin vermis (little worm), refers to the insect-derived kermes red whose colour it was originally mistaken for. Cinnabar is toxic: the mercury content leaches slowly out of the finished pigment and was a recognised occupational hazard for the craftsmen who prepared it. Despite this, the colour is irreplaceable. The Chinese red seal paste tradition uses specifically cinnabar-based formulations because no other red has the same depth of saturation, the same durability over centuries, or the same specific warm-orange cast.

The principal historical sources of Chinese cinnabar were mines in Guizhou Province (particularly the Wanshan mines, which were worked continuously for more than a thousand years) and Hunan Province. Both regions produce high-grade red cinnabar that can be ground and refined into pigment of museum-quality fineness. Traditional Chinese medicine also used cinnabar orally, in small doses, as a supposed treatment for various conditions — a use that is now recognised as toxic but that persisted into the twentieth century.

The seal tradition

The Chinese seal (yin) is a small personal stamp, typically carved in soft stone (soapstone, particularly from Qingtian in Zhejiang or Shoushan in Fujian), that an individual uses to mark documents and artworks in lieu of — or in addition to — a written signature. A serious calligrapher or painter owns several seals, each used in different contexts: the personal name seal, the studio name seal, the auspicious phrase seal, the collector's seal that is applied when acquiring another artist's work. A Qing-dynasty hand scroll might carry dozens of collector's seals along its length, each recording a successive owner across two or three centuries — a kind of visible provenance chain that is one of the distinctive features of the Chinese painting tradition.

The seal is pressed into a soft paste of cinnabar pigment bound with oil and a stiffener (traditionally silk fibre or rush), and then onto the document or painting. The specific Chinese red seal paste — still made today by a handful of traditional producers, most notably the Xiling Seal Art Society in Hangzhou (founded 1904) — is a specific formulation whose colour, consistency, and ageing properties are among the most carefully guarded technical traditions in Chinese craft. A good seal paste will hold its colour for centuries without fading, bleeding, or cracking.

红Hong · Festive Red§

Chinese hong: red, specifically the bright celebratory red of the Spring Festival and the wedding.

Where zhusha (§2.8) is the red of the state seal and the official document, hong is the red of the family gathering and the festival. It is the colour of the lanterns hung at New Year, of the red envelopes (hongbao) passed to children and unmarried younger relatives, of the double-happiness paper cuts pasted on the door at weddings, of the bride's dress, of the firecracker paper that litters the courtyard on the morning of the first day of the lunar year, and of the banner above the restaurant door. It is the single most visually dominant colour in Chinese popular life.

The festive use

The Chinese Spring Festival (Chunjie, the lunar new year) is the single most important annual holiday in the Chinese calendar and the single largest annual movement of people on Earth — roughly three billion individual trips are made across China during the traditional spring festival travel period. The festival is organised around fifteen days of family gatherings, meals, visits, and rituals, and the visual signature of the whole period is red. Red paper banners inscribed with couplets (chunlian) are pasted to the sides and top of the front door of every traditional household. A red paper diamond inscribed with the character fu (good fortune), often hung upside-down to punningly indicate that good fortune has "arrived" (dao), is pasted in the centre of the door. Red lanterns hang from the eaves. Children wear new red clothing. Firecrackers, traditionally wrapped in red paper, are set off to drive away the mythical beast Nian for whom the new year is named.

The origin of the Chinese red symbolism is pre-Buddhist and possibly pre-Confucian. The earliest associations appear in the Warring States and Han periods, when red was already identified with the south, with fire (in the wuxing system), with summer, and with joy. By the Tang dynasty the wedding red had taken its modern form — Chinese brides traditionally wear red, not white, and a Chinese wedding is almost entirely colour-coded red, from the bride's dress to the groom's sash to the envelopes of cash given by guests to the paper cut decorations on the walls of the reception hall. The Western white wedding dress, imported in the twentieth century, has partially displaced the traditional red in modern urban China but has not displaced the underlying cultural association.

The hongbao

The red envelope (hongbao) is a specific festive object: a small rectangular paper envelope, printed in red and often decorated with gold auspicious characters, used to give cash gifts at the Spring Festival, at weddings, at birthdays, and at other celebratory moments. The tradition of giving money in red wrapping is documented from at least the Tang dynasty and possibly earlier; the specific modern envelope format dates to the printing industries of the late Qing. The hongbao is passed by older family members to unmarried younger relatives at the lunar new year — a form of blessing that is also a practical cash transfer — and the custom has proven sufficiently robust that it has been ported intact into the digital age. The Chinese tech companies WeChat and Alipay both introduced digital hongbao features in the 2010s, and the digital versions now circulate at a volume of tens of billions per year in the days around the lunar new year.

2.10

漆器Qiqi · Han Lacquerware§

Chinese qiqi: lacquerware, specifically the red-and-black-and-gold tradition that reaches its classical form in the Han dynasty. The role slot for DARK.

The dark role of the collection is the red-and-gold-on-black lacquer of the Han dynasty — a tradition that was already three thousand years old when it was buried in the Mawangdui tombs in 168 BCE and that continues unbroken through the Ming carved-lacquer tradition into the present day. Chinese lacquer is the parent of the Japanese urushi tradition (Japan chapter §1.10), and the two traditions share a single plant source, a single technical basis, and much of a two-thousand-year craft lineage.

Material & the two lineages

Chinese lacquer (qi) is made, like its Japanese counterpart, from the sap of Toxicodendron vernicifluum, the lacquer tree, which is native to East Asia and has been tapped for its sap in China since the Neolithic period. The Hemudu culture of coastal Zhejiang, active around 7,000 years ago, produced the earliest surviving Chinese lacquered objects — a red lacquered wooden bowl excavated in 1978 at the Hemudu site remains one of the oldest worked-lacquer objects in the world. The Japanese Jōmon-period lacquer tradition (Japan chapter §1.10) is roughly contemporary, and it is not clear whether the two traditions arose independently or whether an earlier shared origin connects them. What is clear is that by the early historical period both China and Japan had fully developed lacquer craft traditions, and that the technical vocabulary of the two has continued to cross-pollinate for three thousand years.

The specific Han-dynasty lacquer palette — black ground with red painted decoration and occasional gold highlights — is the canonical form of early Chinese lacquer and the direct ancestor of the Japanese Heian and Kamakura lacquer traditions. The decoration is painted with a fine brush in cinnabar-pigmented red lacquer (zhuqi) on a ground of iron-oxide-black lacquer, and the best Han pieces carry images of dragons, phoenixes, immortals, and scenes from myth painted with a precision that survives perfectly after two thousand years.

The Mawangdui finds

The single most dramatic Chinese lacquer find of the twentieth century was the excavation of the Mawangdui tombs at Changsha in Hunan Province between 1972 and 1974. The tombs — three in total, belonging to the Marquis of Dai, his wife Lady Dai, and their son — had been sealed in the second century BCE under conditions that preserved their contents almost perfectly. Lady Dai's tomb yielded the most extraordinary single lacquer cache in Chinese archaeology: nearly two hundred painted lacquer vessels, trays, plates, cups, and boxes, all in the classical Han red-and-black palette, many of them bearing inscriptions identifying their original function and their workshop of origin. The finds transformed the study of Han lacquer and are now held at the Hunan Provincial Museum in Changsha, which built a dedicated Mawangdui wing to house them.

The Mawangdui finds also included a series of painted silk banners, a library of early Daoist and medical texts written on silk, a set of musical instruments, and — most famously — the perfectly preserved mummified body of Lady Dai herself, whose internal organs were still soft and whose skin was still elastic when the tomb was opened. The combined material culture of the tomb is one of the most complete extant portraits of elite Han-period life, and the lacquerware is the largest and best-preserved single group of Han-period objects in any medium.

Carved lacquer and the later tradition

A second great Chinese lacquer tradition develops in the Song and Yuan periods and reaches its peak in the Ming. Carved lacquer (diaoqi) is made by building up many layers of lacquer on a wooden or metal core — a hundred layers or more for the finest pieces — and then carving through the stack to produce a deeply cut relief decoration. The specific red variety, called tihong ("carved red"), is made using cinnabar-pigmented lacquer and produces the characteristic deep vermillion-red carved objects that are the signature of Ming imperial lacquer. The Yongle (1402–1424) and Xuande (1426–1435) reigns are the canonical peaks; Ming carved lacquers from these reigns are among the most valuable categories of Chinese art object in the international market.

Representative objects

  • aThe Hemudu lacquered wooden bowl. c. 5000 BCE. Zhejiang Provincial Museum. The oldest surviving Chinese lacquer and among the oldest worked-lacquer objects in the world.
  • bThe Mawangdui lacquerware. Tomb 1, c. 168 BCE. Hunan Provincial Museum, Changsha. The largest and most complete surviving Han-period lacquer group. Hunan Provincial Museum
  • cMing imperial carved red lacquer (tihong) boxes. Yongle and Xuande reigns, fifteenth century. The Palace Museum Beijing, the National Palace Museum Taipei, the British Museum, and the V&A all hold fine examples. V&A search
  • dSong and Yuan lacquer in the Linden-Museum, Stuttgart. One of the finest European collections of early Chinese lacquer.

Sources

  1. iSir Harry Garner — Chinese Lacquer (Faber & Faber, 1979). The foundational English-language scholarly history, still the standard reference.
  2. iiJames C. Y. Watt & Barbara Brennan Ford — East Asian Lacquer: The Florence and Herbert Irving Collection (Metropolitan Museum of Art, 1991). Covers both the Chinese and Japanese traditions in a single integrated catalogue.
  3. iiiRegina Krahl — The Emperor's Private Paradise: Treasures from the Forbidden City (Peabody Essex Museum / Forbidden City Publishing, 2010). Includes the Qianlong-period lacquer at the highest level of Qing imperial craft.
  4. ivMonique Crick (ed.) — Chinese Lacquer from the Jean-Pierre Dubosc Collection (Baur Foundation, Geneva, 2014). A major recent catalogue covering multiple dynasties.
  5. vEugene Y. Wang — Shaping the Lotus Sutra: Buddhist Visual Culture in Medieval China (University of Washington Press, 2005). Includes coverage of Tang-period lacquered Buddhist objects.

Further reading for the China chapter

  1. iRobert Finlay — The Pilgrim Art: Cultures of Porcelain in World History (University of California Press, 2010). The essential modern account of the global diffusion of Chinese porcelain. If one book can be read, this is it.
  2. iiMargaret Medley — The Chinese Potter: A Practical History of Chinese Ceramics (Phaidon, 1976; third edition 1989). The single best English-language survey of the full Chinese ceramic tradition, from Neolithic to Qing.
  3. iiiJessica Rawson — The British Museum Book of Chinese Art (British Museum Press, 1992; second edition 2007). The standard one-volume introduction to the full range of Chinese art, written by the keeper of the British Museum's Chinese collections.
  4. ivJames Cahill — The Compelling Image: Nature and Style in Seventeenth-Century Chinese Painting (Harvard University Press, 1982). Cahill's short essays on Chinese painting are the most accessible entry point into the literati tradition.
  5. vWen Fong — Beyond Representation: Chinese Painting and Calligraphy 8th–14th Century (Yale / Metropolitan Museum, 1992). The authoritative scholarly account of the critical centuries for Chinese painting.
  6. viCraig Clunas — Art in China (Oxford University Press, 1997; second edition 2009). The best modern single-volume history of Chinese art, structured thematically rather than chronologically.
  7. viiCraig Clunas — Superfluous Things: Material Culture and Social Status in Early Modern China (Polity, 1991). The groundbreaking study of late Ming material culture and its relationship to literati taste.
  8. viiiPatricia Buckley Ebrey — The Cambridge Illustrated History of China (Cambridge University Press, 1996; second edition 2010). The standard narrative history.
  9. ixJonathan D. Spence — The Search for Modern China (W. W. Norton, 1990; third edition 2013). The standard English-language modern history.
  10. xValerie Hansen — The Open Empire: A History of China to 1800 (W. W. Norton, 2000; second edition 2015). A strong alternative to Ebrey with more emphasis on trade and material culture.
  11. xiJoseph Needham (ed.) — Science and Civilisation in China (Cambridge University Press, 1954–present), many volumes. The monumental reference work on Chinese scientific and technical history, including the papermaking (V:1), ceramic technology (V:12), and textile dyeing volumes.
  12. xiiMa Huan — Ying-yai Sheng-lan: The Overall Survey of the Ocean's Shores (1433). English translation by J. V. G. Mills (Cambridge University Press / Hakluyt Society, 1970). The primary Chinese source on Zheng He's early-fifteenth-century voyages and their trade contacts.
  13. xiiiPère François Xavier d'Entrecolles — Letters of 1712 and 1722 from Jingdezhen. Public domain. Stanislas Julien's 1856 French translation is the standard scholarly edition.
  14. xivVictoria Finlay — Colour: Travels Through the Paintbox (Bibliothèque §0.1). Finlay's blue chapter covers the Chinese cobalt trade; her red chapter covers cinnabar; her yellow chapter covers imperial yellow.
  15. xvMichel Pastoureau — Yellow (2019) and Blue (2000), both Princeton University Press. Pastoureau's European focus provides the comparative frame for the Chinese imperial yellow and for the global cobalt lineage.
