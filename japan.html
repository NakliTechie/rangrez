Preface

Japan is the world's most disciplined chromatic tradition. Where the Persian palette is a story of pigments carried outwards along trade routes, and the Chinese palette is a story of imperial codification over three millennia, the Japanese palette is a story of restraint — of a culture that took the full colour spectrum it had inherited from Tang China, subjected it to nearly a thousand years of philosophical editing, and produced at the far end a vocabulary in which the most prized colours are muted, desaturated, seasonal, and often deliberately incomplete.

The editing happens in waves. The Heian court in the ninth through twelfth centuries codifies the twelve-layered kimono (jūnihitoe) and the seasonal colour combinations that accompany each moment of the year; The Pillow Book of Sei Shōnagon and The Tale of Genji of Lady Murasaki are effectively the world's first extended writing on colour as social code. The Kamakura and Muromachi periods bring Zen Buddhism from China and the Sinified ink-wash aesthetic with it; the tea ceremony, codified by Sen no Rikyū in the sixteenth century, turns the appreciation of a single rough bowl into a complete aesthetic discipline. The Edo shogunate (1603–1868) then enforces a two-and-a-half-century set of sumptuary laws that restrict what colours the merchant and artisan classes are permitted to wear — and in response, the townspeople of Edo develop the shijūhasshoku, the "forty-eight browns and a hundred greys," a chromatic system that extracts maximum expressive range from the narrow band of muted tones legally available to them.

The ten palettes in this chapter follow that historical arc. Three role slots hold the anchors of the tradition: the unbleached raw paper of kinari, the carbon-black sumi ink that writes on it, and the deep red-black urushi lacquer that coats the finer objects of the home. Six overrides carry the canonical seasonal and material colours — the cherry blossom that announces spring, the whisked green of tea, the Edo indigo of the workman's coat, the decayed leaf of late November, the fresh rush-mat floor, the safflower red of the geisha's lip. The tenth slot is the modern closer: muji, the cleanest white, taken from the brand that — more consciously than any other twentieth-century commercial enterprise — tried to distil a thousand years of Japanese chromatic restraint into a line of products for the post-industrial global consumer.

A Japanese chronology

  • c. 14,000 BCEThe Jōmon period begins. The earliest pottery in East Asia is made on the Japanese archipelago, roughly four thousand years before pottery appears anywhere in the Fertile Crescent.
  • 710 CEThe Nara period begins. The imperial capital is fixed at Heijō-kyō (modern Nara) on a Chinese grid. Buddhism, tea, ink, and Tang Chinese colour vocabulary all enter Japan in this period.
  • 752 CEThe Great Buddha of Tōdai-ji is dedicated at Nara — the largest bronze casting in Japanese history and the centrepiece of a monastery that still stands.
  • 794 CEThe capital moves to Heian-kyō (modern Kyoto). The Heian period will last until 1185 and produce the aesthetic vocabulary on which all later Japanese colour tradition is built.
  • c. 1002Sei Shōnagon writes The Pillow Book (Makura no Sōshi), the first substantial literary document of Heian court chromatic sensibility.
  • c. 1010Lady Murasaki Shikibu writes The Tale of Genji (Genji monogatari), arguably the world's first psychological novel. Its readings of colour as social and seasonal code are still the foundation of Japanese chromatic scholarship.
  • 1191The Zen monk Eisai returns from Song China with tea seeds and begins the planting of tea in Japan. The tea ceremony will emerge from this initial introduction over the next four centuries.
  • 1336–1573The Muromachi period. Zen Buddhism becomes central to elite culture. Ink-wash painting (suiboku-ga) reaches its Japanese height under Sesshū Tōyō (1420–1506). Tea, dry gardens, Noh theatre, and the wabi-sabi aesthetic all crystallise.
  • 1522–1591Sen no Rikyū codifies the wabi-cha tea ceremony under the patronage of Toyotomi Hideyoshi. His death by ritual suicide, ordered by Hideyoshi, marks the moment the tea aesthetic becomes a serious political matter.
  • 1603Tokugawa Ieyasu is named shogun. The Edo period begins, and with it two and a half centuries of rigid sumptuary law restricting the colours, fabrics, and fashions permitted to each social class.
  • 17th–19th c.The shijūhasshoku — forty-eight browns and one hundred greys — develops among the Edo townspeople as a response to sumptuary restriction. The ukiyo-e print tradition flourishes in parallel (Hokusai, Hiroshige, Utamaro).
  • 1868The Meiji Restoration ends the Edo shogunate and opens Japan to the West. Western chromatic norms enter the country in a single generation; the traditional colours do not disappear but are repositioned as traditional rather than current.
  • 1906Okakura Kakuzō, writing in English for an American audience, publishes The Book of Tea. It remains the single most influential Western-language introduction to Japanese aesthetics.
  • 1926Yanagi Sōetsu and a small group of colleagues launch the mingei (folk craft) movement, which will underwrite every twentieth-century Japanese design revival from Hamada Shōji's pottery to the founding of Muji.
  • 1933Tanizaki Jun'ichirō publishes In Praise of Shadows (In'ei Raisan), a short essay arguing that the Japanese aesthetic preference for dim, muted, shadowed beauty is incompatible with the bright electrical lighting of Western modernity. It becomes the single most-quoted Western-language reference on the Japanese palette.
  • 1980The retail brand Mujirushi Ryōhin (Muji) is founded by Seiji Tsutsumi as a deliberate austerity counterpoint to the brand-logo consumerism of late-1970s Japan.
  • 2009 / 2013 / 2014Three successive UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage inscriptions for Japanese material tradition: washoku (traditional cuisine, 2013), the art of washi papermaking (2014), and the Gion Matsuri and other festival traditions.
1.1

無印Muji · The Cleanest White§

Japanese mujirushi: no-brand, no-mark. The role slot for LIGHT.

The light role of the collection is taken not from a historical pigment but from a twentieth-century brand — specifically from the particular desaturated, almost blue-shifted white that the retail chain Muji has used since its founding in 1980 as the ground colour of its packaging, its stores, and its house philosophy. It is the cleanest white in the collection because Muji's chief art director set out deliberately to make it so.

The brand as aesthetic argument

Muji — formally Mujirushi Ryōhin, literally "no-brand quality goods" — was founded in December 1980 as a subsidiary of the Seiyu supermarket chain under the direction of Seiji Tsutsumi, a businessman, poet, and cultural patron who was deliberately positioning the new line as an austerity counterpoint to the logo-driven consumerism of late-Showa Japan. The original Muji catalogue listed forty items, most of them household goods: a broken-rice pasta, a pair of unbleached cotton socks, a recycled-paper notebook. The chromatic register was fixed from the beginning at unbleached cream and off-white, with occasional warm brown or soft grey accents — a register that has not meaningfully changed in the four and a half decades since.

From 2001 the brand's art director has been Kenya Hara, a designer and author whose books — Designing Design (2007) and White (2009) — are the closest thing the contemporary design world has to a formal manifesto for Muji's chromatic commitments. Hara's argument, sustained across both books, is that white is not a colour but a condition: the absence of intervention, the state of being left alone. The Muji store, in Hara's framing, is a space in which a thousand years of Japanese aesthetic restraint has been translated into a retail vocabulary that can speak to the post-industrial global consumer without needing to be explained.

The longer lineage

Muji did not appear from nowhere. Its direct aesthetic ancestor is the mingei movement launched by Yanagi Sōetsu in the mid-1920s, which argued that the anonymous handmade objects of ordinary Japanese craftsmen — bowls, baskets, clothes, tools — possessed a beauty that was being lost to industrialisation. Yanagi's The Unknown Craftsman (first English edition 1972) is the theoretical source text, and the Japan Folk Crafts Museum (Nihon Mingeikan) in Tokyo, which he founded in 1936, is the institutional legacy.

The deeper lineage runs back to wabi-sabi itself — the Muromachi-era aesthetic of imperfection, impermanence, and incompleteness that the tea masters codified in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries (see §1.5 MATCHA below). Muji is, in a sense, the wabi-sabi tradition translated into a mass-produced line of socks and pasta. That this translation survives at all — that a commercial brand can carry a thousand-year aesthetic discipline without immediately collapsing it into kitsch — is one of the more remarkable facts of twentieth-century Japanese material culture.

1.2

生成りKinari · Unbleached§

Japanese kinari: "as-grown," unbleached. The role slot for WARM.

The warm anchor of the collection is the soft cream-ivory of unbleached natural fibre — raw silk, raw cotton, raw hemp, or the mulberry-bark paper of the Japanese washi tradition. Kinari is a canonical shijūhasshoku colour name, and it signals not a dye but an absence: the specific warm off-white that a natural fibre takes when it has been spun, woven, or pulped but never subjected to bleach or mordant.

Material

The specific substance that defines kinari for most modern Japanese readers is washi — the handmade paper that has been produced in Japan since the seventh century and that was inscribed on the UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2014. Washi is made principally from three plants: kōzo (paper mulberry), gampi (a wild shrub of the Thymelaeaceae family), and mitsumata (an ornamental relative). The pulping, beating, and sheet-forming process is traditionally done in winter, in cold mountain streams, because the cold water slows microbial growth and keeps the long fibres from breaking down. The resulting paper is strong enough to be used as a substrate for joinery, lightweight enough to be used for shoji screens, and chromatic enough — when left unbleached — to carry the entire warm end of the Japanese paper tradition.

The three main surviving traditional washi regions — Hosokawa washi (Saitama), Sekishū-banshi (Shimane), and Hon-minoshi (Gifu) — are all named in the UNESCO inscription, and all three produce paper that sits within the canonical kinari range.

History

Kinari-coloured paper is the ground of Japanese literary civilisation. The earliest surviving Tale of Genji manuscripts, the poetry anthologies of the Heian court, the Buddhist sutras copied at Nara, the Edo merchant ledgers, the haiku notebooks of Matsuo Bashō — all of them are written on washi that falls within the kinari range. When Hon'ami Kōetsu (1558–1637), the great Rinpa-school calligrapher, produced his famous poetry scrolls in collaboration with the painter Tawaraya Sōtatsu, the background of those scrolls was a dyed or decorated washi whose undertone is specifically kinari.

The word itself has an everyday counterpart in the textile world. The unbleached ramie and hemp used for summer kimono linings, the raw silk used for formal mounts on hanging scrolls, the cotton of traditional tabi socks — all of them are called kinari when they are left in their natural state, and the word carries an implicit approval: left alone, not interfered with, correct in its as-grown condition.

1.3

墨Sumi · Ink§

Japanese sumi: ink, specifically the solid ink stick. The role slot for MONO.

The monochrome role of the collection is the carbon-black Japanese ink stick — a compressed bar of soot and animal glue that is ground against a stone with a little water to produce the specific deep, slightly warm, slightly blue-black ink in which the entire Japanese tradition of calligraphy, ink-wash painting, haiku draft, and sutra copy has been recorded since at least the eighth century.

Material

Japanese sumi is made from two kinds of soot. Shōen-boku uses soot collected from the slow burning of red pine, producing a slightly cool, slightly blue-black ink historically preferred for calligraphy. Yuen-boku uses soot collected from the burning of vegetable oils — rape-seed, sesame, camellia — producing a slightly warmer, slightly brown-black ink historically preferred for painting. Both kinds of soot are mixed with an animal-derived glue (nikawa, made from deer or cow hides), kneaded into a dough, moulded into a small rectangular bar, and dried slowly for weeks. The drying is the step that determines the quality: a well-dried stick will release pigment cleanly and evenly for decades. A poorly dried stick will crumble.

Ink-making arrived in Japan from China around the sixth century and was systematised in Nara after the Buddhist monk Kūkai (Kōbō Daishi, 774–835) is traditionally credited with bringing improved techniques back from Tang China in the early ninth century. Nara has been the centre of Japanese sumi production ever since. The venerable firm Kobaien, founded in 1577, still makes ink by hand in Nara using recipes substantially unchanged since the Muromachi period, and a stick from their highest grade is among the most expensive single objects in the Japanese craft world.

History & aesthetic

Sumi entered Japan together with Chinese writing itself — meaning that the ink is not merely the medium of Japanese writing but is coeval with the tradition. Every surviving sheet of Japanese writing from the eighth century onwards is a sumi object. The eighth-century Buddhist sutras at Tōdai-ji, the Heian-period poetry anthologies on their kinari grounds (§1.2), the Kamakura-period dry-brush landscapes of Sesshū Tōyō, the Rinpa-school calligraphy of Hon'ami Kōetsu, the haiku manuscripts of Bashō, and the twentieth-century calligraphic work of Morita Shiryū — all of them use ground sumi as their only pigment.

The crucial technical point for the palette is that sumi is not a single black. Depending on how much water is used in the grinding, it runs from a nearly solid carbon-black at the densest dilution to a pale silver-grey at the lightest — and the Japanese ink-wash tradition (suiboku-ga) is built precisely on the gradation between those extremes. A single brush-stroke might carry five different tones across its length, and the great ink painters are judged largely on how precisely they can control the gradation. Sesshū's Haboku Landscape for Sōen (1495) is the canonical demonstration.

1.4

桜Sakura · Cherry Blossom§

Japanese sakura: cherry blossom, specifically Prunus × yedoensis.

Sakura is a pale pink — more specifically, a whisper of pink so faint that at mid-distance a cherry tree in full bloom reads as pure white against a grey Kyoto sky, and only resolves into its chromatic identity when a single petal lands on a dark sleeve or a wet stone. It is the single most emotionally loaded colour in the Japanese tradition, and its meaning is inseparable from its brevity: the canonical Somei Yoshino cultivar blooms for roughly seven to ten days each spring, and the cultural practice of hanami — cherry-blossom viewing — is structured around that brevity.

Material & cultivar

The Japanese cherry is not a single plant but a family of more than two hundred cultivars, most of them ornamental rather than fruit-bearing. The one that dominates both the popular imagination and the actual street plantings of every Japanese city is Prunus × yedoensis, the Somei Yoshino, a sterile hybrid that was bred in the Somei district of Edo (modern-day Toshima in Tokyo) in the mid-nineteenth century and that has since been propagated by grafting onto every cherry-bearing hillside in the country. Because all Somei Yoshino trees are clones, they bloom in synchrony — which is why the cherry-blossom forecast (sakura zensen) issued annually by the Japan Meteorological Agency is so precise: a single front rolls north from Okinawa in late March and reaches Hokkaido by early May.

The petals themselves are a specific very pale pink that depends on the age of the individual flower. A newly opened Somei Yoshino blossom is almost pure white with the faintest warm undertone; over the three to four days of its life the pink deepens very slightly and then the petal falls. The canonical sakura colour in the Japanese traditional palette is taken from a blossom in the middle of this arc.

The cultural weight

Cherry-blossom viewing is documented in Japan from at least the Nara period, but it becomes a formal court practice in the Heian period, at which point it is codified in the diaries, letters, and poetry anthologies of the aristocracy. The Kokin Wakashū (c. 905), the first imperial poetry anthology, already treats the cherry blossom as a major subject, and the Tale of Genji mentions it in dozens of scenes. By the Edo period hanami has become a general popular practice: merchants, labourers, samurai, and courtesans all went out under the cherry trees together, carrying lunch boxes and sake.

The emotional register of the cherry blossom in Japanese culture is captured by the phrase mono no aware, which the eighteenth-century scholar Motoori Norinaga identified as the central aesthetic-emotional principle of the Tale of Genji and, by extension, of Japanese literary culture as a whole. The phrase is difficult to translate — "the pathos of things," "the sadness of things," "an awareness of impermanence" — but the cherry blossom is its single clearest visual emblem. The flower is beautiful because it is brief; its brevity is what makes it beautiful; and looking at it for a week each spring is one of the oldest continuous aesthetic practices in the Japanese repertoire.

This emotional register has also been used politically. During the 1930s and 1940s, Japanese militarists appropriated the cherry blossom as a symbol of the soldier who dies young for the emperor — an appropriation that Emiko Ohnuki-Tierney documents in careful detail in her 2002 book Kamikaze, Cherry Blossoms, and Nationalisms, and that complicates any contemporary reading of the flower. The complication is part of the material and cannot be cleanly separated from it.

抹茶Matcha · Whisked Tea§

Japanese matcha: powdered green tea, whisked in a bowl with hot water.

Matcha is a specific bright saturated green — almost the opposite of the muted browns and greys that dominate the rest of the Japanese palette — and its cultural weight comes entirely from its ritual context. For four centuries it has been the central colour of the Japanese tea ceremony (chanoyu), the single most elaborate aesthetic discipline the country has produced, and almost every Japanese reader encounters the colour first in the form of a specific ceremonial moment: a lacquer caddy opened, a bamboo scoop lifted, a small heap of intensely green powder tapped into a dark tea bowl, and the silence before the whisking begins.

Material

Matcha is made from tencha, a specific cultivar of Camellia sinensis whose plants are shaded for roughly three weeks before harvest. The shading forces the plant to produce more chlorophyll and more theanine (the amino acid that gives tea its savoury umami character) and less of the bitter polyphenols that characterise unshaded green tea. The shaded leaves are steamed briefly to halt fermentation, then dried, de-veined, and stone-ground into a fine powder. The grinding is done slowly — a traditional granite mill produces roughly forty grams of matcha per hour — and produces a powder fine enough to hold in suspension when whisked with hot water, which is the technical prerequisite for the ceremony's characteristic froth.

The historical centre of matcha production is Uji, a small city just south of Kyoto, where tea has been cultivated since the Kamakura period and where the finest modern matcha still comes from. The Uji producers Marukyu-Koyamaen, Ippodo, and Horii Shichimeien are the canonical sources for the highest-grade ceremonial matcha and have been family businesses for several centuries.

History

Tea arrived in Japan in the ninth century with returning Buddhist monks, but the specific whisked-matcha form of the drink arrived in 1191 with the Zen monk Eisai, who returned from Song China carrying tea seeds and a treatise — the Kissa Yōjōki, "Drinking Tea for Health" — that is the first Japanese work on tea. Eisai planted the first tea garden at Kōzan-ji, the temple still standing in the hills north of Kyoto. The whisked-powder preparation that Eisai brought was the standard form of tea in Song China at the time; in China it was subsequently replaced by the loose-leaf steeped preparation we now associate with Chinese tea, but in Japan the Song-dynasty form was preserved and refined for eight centuries.

The tea ceremony as a formal aesthetic discipline is codified in the sixteenth century by Sen no Rikyū (1522–1591), who served as tea master first to the warlord Oda Nobunaga and then to his successor Toyotomi Hideyoshi. Rikyū's aesthetic principle was wabi-cha — a form of tea practice that deliberately rejected the ostentatious Chinese-style utensils then fashionable at court in favour of rough Korean-made tea bowls, bamboo utensils, thatched tea-house architecture, and a general register of deliberate rustic imperfection. Hideyoshi, whose own taste ran more toward the ostentatious, eventually ordered Rikyū to commit ritual suicide in 1591 over a dispute whose precise content is still debated by historians, but Rikyū's aesthetic survived intact and became the basis of the three modern tea schools — Omotesenke, Urasenke, and Mushakōjisenke — that still trace their lineage directly back to him through his descendants.

The canonical quote

The single most-quoted Western-language characterisation of the tea ceremony comes from Okakura Kakuzō's The Book of Tea, written in English and published in Boston in 1906 as an attempt to explain the Japanese aesthetic to an American audience in the wake of the Russo-Japanese War. Okakura argued that the tea ceremony was neither entertainment nor recreation but a complete aesthetic discipline that he called Teaism, and he defined it in a sentence that has been in continuous circulation in English-language writing on Japan for well over a century.

Teaism is a cult founded on the adoration of the beautiful. Okakura Kakuzō, The Book of Tea, 1906. Public domain.

紺Kon · Deep Indigo§

Japanese kon: deep indigo, specifically the darkest shade in the traditional Japanese indigo range.

Kon is the near-black deep indigo of the Edo workman's coat, the noren curtain that hangs at the entrance of a traditional shop, the sashiko-stitched farm jacket, and the short-sleeved summer yukata. It is the single most visually dominant colour in the everyday material culture of pre-modern Japan — so dominant that the late-nineteenth-century visitor Lafcadio Hearn repeatedly described Japan as a country whose prevailing colour, seen from any village street, was blue.

Material

Japanese indigo (ai) is produced from Polygonum tinctorium, a buckwheat relative native to East Asia that is distinct from the Indigofera tinctoria cultivated in India, Africa, and the Caribbean. The two plants produce chemically identical indigo dye but through slightly different agricultural and fermentation paths. The Japanese process ferments the harvested ai leaves for roughly one hundred days into a dark composted mass called sukumo, which is then mixed with wood-ash lye, wheat bran, and sake to build a live fermentation vat. The vat is tended like a sourdough — fed, warmed, and stirred daily — and produces dye continuously for months. The cloth is dipped into the vat, pulled out oxidising to green in the air, then to blue, and dipped again; each dip deepens the colour, and the deepest shade — kon — requires twenty or more dippings.

The historical centre of Japanese indigo production is Tokushima Prefecture on the island of Shikoku, where the crop was cultivated on the fertile floodplains of the Yoshino River from at least the fifteenth century onwards. Awa-ai, the indigo of the old Awa Province (modern Tokushima), became famous throughout Japan in the Edo period as the highest-grade source, and a small number of traditional sukumo producers in Tokushima are still working today with unbroken family lineages going back to the early nineteenth century.

History & social use

Indigo's dominance in Edo-period everyday life is partly aesthetic and partly regulatory. The Tokugawa sumptuary laws that restricted the merchant and artisan classes to muted colours specifically permitted indigo — it was considered a sufficiently humble dye — while restricting the reds, purples, and bright yellows reserved for the samurai and aristocratic classes. The result was that an entire class of people, comprising the great majority of the Japanese urban and rural population, dressed in indigo as a matter of legal compulsion as well as cultural preference.

Three specific Edo-period uses carry the colour into the modern palette. First, sashiko: the running-stitch reinforcement technique used to strengthen farm clothing and fishermen's coats, traditionally stitched in white cotton thread on a deep indigo ground. Second, noren: the split cloth curtain hung at the entrance of a shop or restaurant, typically dyed with the shop's crest in white on a kon ground. Third, happi: the short straight-sleeved festival coat, usually worn with the shop's or the temple's mon crest on the back in white against kon. All three traditions are still alive in modern Japan, and all three are the principal reasons that a visitor to any older Japanese neighbourhood still sees the colour everywhere.

Lafcadio Hearn, the Irish-Greek writer who moved to Japan in 1890 and spent the last fourteen years of his life there, remarked in his travel essays that the single most visually striking feature of the Japanese country was the ubiquity of the indigo work-coat — he saw it, he wrote, on every farmer in every field and every porter in every station, to the point that the landscape of working Japan was effectively blue. Hearn's essays are public domain and remain among the most vivid Western descriptions of Meiji-era everyday colour.

1.7

朽葉Kuchiba · Decayed Leaf§

Japanese kuchiba: decayed leaf, rotted leaf. A canonical shijūhasshoku colour name.

Kuchiba is a specific warm brown, slightly dulled, slightly orange-shifted — the colour of a fallen ginkgo leaf that has lain for several days on wet temple stone in late November, after the bright yellow of its first fall has begun to fade toward its final dry rust. It is one of the canonical shijūhasshoku colour names, and it signals the end of the autumn colour cycle in the Japanese traditional palette: no longer the vivid kōyō (autumn-leaf) reds and golds of the viewing season, but the deeper, duller, more contemplative brown that comes when the leaf has finished its performance.

The colour within the tradition

The Japanese traditional colour vocabulary — particularly in its Heian and Edo codifications — is organised around the seasons with an almost mathematical precision. Each season has its canonical colours; each month of each season has its sub-palette; each flower, fruit, and weather phenomenon has a colour name associated with it. Kuchiba sits near the end of autumn, after the peak of momijigari (autumn-leaf viewing, the autumnal counterpart to spring hanami) but before the pure whites of winter. It is a colour of transition and decline — of acknowledging that the red maples have passed their peak and that the bright yellows of the ginkgo have begun to darken.

The shijūhasshoku system — literally "the forty-eight teas and the hundred mouses" — is a late-Edo extension of the older Heian court colour tradition. The Edo merchant class, legally restricted from wearing bright colours under the shogunate's sumptuary regulations (see §1.6 KON), turned the restriction into a competitive aesthetic: if the only colours permitted were browns and greys, they would invent as many browns and greys as possible and assign them elaborate literary names. The result is a palette of at least 148 distinct colour names in the historical record, each carrying a specific reference — to a plant, a season, a literary allusion, or a dyer's workshop. Kuchiba is one of the older names in the set, attested in Heian court sources and carried forward into the Edo shijūhasshoku intact.

Representative sites

The two canonical sites for the specific experience of kuchiba are the Kyoto temples where ginkgo and maple are combined with moss and stone, and where the late-November viewing period is the most intense. Tōfuku-ji, a Rinzai Zen temple at the southern edge of the Higashiyama district, has been the single most famous autumn-leaf viewing site in Japan since the fifteenth century and draws the largest crowds of the Kyoto autumn. Eikan-dō, further north in the same district, is the other canonical destination. In both temples, the kuchiba tone appears underfoot on the stone pavements and moss beds, not overhead in the trees.

畳Tatami · Rush Mat§

Japanese tatami: the traditional rush floor mat.

Tatami is a pale yellow-green fading slowly to straw-gold — the specific surface colour of the woven soft-rush matting that has been the standard floor covering of Japanese domestic architecture since at least the Muromachi period. A fresh tatami mat, newly laid, carries a distinct faint green-gold that is among the most characteristic interior colours in any traditional Japanese space; the colour deepens and warms gradually over several years into a pale wheat-brown before the mat is replaced.

Material & construction

A traditional tatami mat is built in three layers. The base is a thick compressed core of rice straw (tatami-doko), roughly five centimetres thick and heavy enough that one mat requires two people to carry. Over this is laid a woven surface of igusa (soft rush, Juncus effusus), held together in a tight over-under weave that produces the characteristic grain of the finished mat. The edge is bound with a fabric border (heri) which in classical interiors carried family crests and which in modern tatami is usually a plain dark cloth.

Igusa cultivation is labour-intensive and has declined sharply in Japan since the 1970s as cheaper Chinese imports and synthetic substitutes have taken the mass market. Kumamoto Prefecture, on the island of Kyushu, remains the principal domestic producer, and the handful of traditional makers who still harvest, dry, and weave igusa by hand are among the most specialised remaining Japanese craftsmen.

The chromatic life of a tatami mat follows a predictable curve. In the first weeks after installation the surface is a vivid, almost fluorescent green-yellow with a strong herbal scent. Within six months the green has softened into a paler yellow-gold. Within three years the surface has become a warm straw colour. By five years the mat is visibly worn and the family will typically replace it. A traditional household would re-cover the mats (replacing only the igusa surface while retaining the straw core) every five to seven years as part of the seasonal domestic cycle.

Architectural role

The tatami mat is not only a floor covering but the basic dimensional module of Japanese traditional architecture. Rooms are measured in tatami: a four-and-a-half-mat room (yojōhan, literally "four-and-a-half mats") is a small intimate space and is the canonical size of a traditional tea room. A six-mat room is the standard size of a middle-class domestic living room. An eight-mat room is a larger formal space. The mat itself has standardised dimensions that vary slightly by region — the Kyoto standard is 955 × 1910 mm, the Tokyo standard slightly smaller at 880 × 1760 mm — but the ratio is always 1:2, so that mats can be combined into rectangular rooms without leaving gaps. The entire planning module of traditional Japanese houses is organised around this ratio.

The Katsura Detached Palace in Kyoto, built between 1615 and 1662 for the Hachijō princely family, is the canonical expression of tatami-module architecture at its highest sophistication. The palace's principal rooms, their dimensions, their proportions, and their chromatic relationships to the surrounding garden are all derived from the mat as the fundamental unit. The twentieth-century architect Bruno Taut called Katsura, on first seeing it in 1933, the most beautiful building in the world — and the statement has been cited so often since that it has become almost a cliché, but the building continues to earn it.

紅Beni · Safflower Red§

Japanese beni: red, specifically the luminous safflower-derived red used historically as cosmetic pigment.

Beni is a specific luminous red — slightly cool, slightly coral, with a distinctive almost-iridescent surface quality that shifts toward green at certain angles — produced by the laborious fermentation and distillation of the flowers of the safflower plant, Carthamus tinctorius. It was the most expensive dye used in pre-modern Japan, worth several times its weight in gold at its peak, and it was reserved almost entirely for cosmetic use: the lip of the geisha, the cheek of the courtesan, the ceremonial makeup of the Heian court and of the modern kabuki stage.

Material

Safflower is a thistle-like annual native to western Asia whose florets contain two distinct pigments: a yellow (carthamin) that dissolves readily in water, and a red (carthamine) that dissolves only in alkali. The Japanese extraction process, refined over centuries, exploits this difference: the yellow is washed out of the harvested petals first and discarded, leaving behind a concentrated red residue that is then pressed into small cakes, dried, and ground. The yield is minuscule — roughly one kilogram of finished beni pigment from one hundred kilograms of fresh safflower flowers — which is the reason for the historical price.

The traditional centre of Japanese safflower cultivation is the Mogami River valley in Yamagata Prefecture, where the crop was grown from at least the sixteenth century for shipment down the Mogami and across the Sea of Japan to Kyoto and Osaka. The specific colour produced by Yamagata safflower — the Mogami beni — was considered the finest in Japan and commanded the highest prices. A small number of traditional producers in Yamagata still grow safflower and produce beni using the traditional method, though the scale is now a few kilograms per year rather than the historical tonnage.

A curious optical property of concentrated beni pigment is that at very high concentration — as when it is painted thickly on a small surface like a lip — it develops a surface iridescence that shifts toward green when viewed obliquely. This effect, called sasairo-beni (bamboo-leaf beni), was considered the highest expression of the pigment and the standard by which geisha lip makeup was judged. A properly applied sasairo-beni lip reads red at direct viewing but shows a green shimmer at the edge.

History & use

Beni was used in three main contexts. First, as cosmetic pigment for lip, cheek, and occasionally eye makeup — the dominant use throughout the Edo period and into the Meiji. Second, as textile dye for the linings of kimono and for undergarments, where the colour was valued specifically because it would not be seen publicly and therefore carried a particular intimacy; the Japanese aesthetic concept of the concealed luxury is strongly associated with beni-dyed silk linings. Third, as pigment in the ukiyo-e woodblock print tradition, where a specific form of the pigment (beni-e) was used in early eighteenth-century prints before being displaced by cheaper synthetic reds in the mid-nineteenth century.

The Isehan cosmetic company, a Tokyo-based firm founded in 1825, is the single surviving producer of traditional Japanese beni cosmetic. Their small shop in Minami-Aoyama, which opened in 2006 and houses the Beni Museum, is the only place in the world where one can see the traditional pigment being made, sold, and applied in its historical form.

漆Urushi · Lacquer§

Japanese urushi: lacquer, specifically the cured sap of Toxicodendron vernicifluum. The role slot for DARK, and the keystone of the chapter.

Urushi is the keystone of Japanese material culture. The deep, almost-black, slightly warm red-brown of cured lacquer is the surface that coats the finest Japanese objects of every period — the tea caddies of the Muromachi tea masters, the armour of the Sengoku warlords, the writing boxes of the Heian court, the repaired ceramics of the kintsugi tradition, and the export wares that so impressed seventeenth-century Europe that they gave the English language the verb "to japan." It is the single Japanese craft that has most clearly travelled outwards and reshaped the material expectations of other cultures, and its technical mastery is among the most demanding in the entire global craft tradition.

Material & chemistry

Urushi is the refined sap of Toxicodendron vernicifluum, the lacquer tree, a relative of poison ivy native to East Asia and cultivated in Japan since at least the Jōmon period — radiocarbon-dated lacquered combs and vessels from Jōmon sites in Hokkaidō push the tradition back to roughly 7,000 BCE, making it one of the oldest continuous craft technologies in the world. The active chemical component is urushiol, a phenolic compound that polymerises in the presence of humidity and a specific laccase enzyme to form an extraordinarily hard, water-resistant, heat-resistant, acid-resistant surface. Urushiol is also a potent contact allergen that produces severe dermatitis in most non-habituated humans, which is the principal reason that the craft of lacquer application has always been confined to a small professional class who develop tolerance through gradual controlled exposure.

The sap is collected by making shallow horizontal cuts in the bark of a mature tree (at least ten years old) and catching the slow-flowing grey-white liquid that weeps out over several hours. A single tree yields roughly one hundred and fifty to two hundred grams of raw sap per year, and the collection effectively destroys the tree — after five years of tapping the tree is cut down. The raw sap is then filtered, heated, stirred, and homogenised into a usable lacquer, at which point it can be pigmented with vermillion (for the classic red-on-black colour scheme), with iron oxide (for the dark brown-black called rō-iro), or with gold and silver powder (for the decorative techniques of maki-e).

The finished lacquer coating on a fine object is built up in many thin layers — twenty to forty is typical for a mid-grade piece, and the finest Wajima lacquerware can carry more than a hundred layers. Each layer is applied, dried in a controlled humidity chamber for one to several days, and then polished smooth with charcoal and water before the next layer is added. A single high-quality lacquer bowl can take a year to complete, and the process cannot be accelerated: the polymerisation chemistry requires specific temperature and humidity and simply refuses to cure outside a narrow range.

The two great regional traditions

Two regional centres dominate the Japanese lacquer tradition. Wajima-nuri, produced in the city of Wajima on the Noto Peninsula of Ishikawa Prefecture, has been in continuous production since at least the fourteenth century and is widely considered the finest everyday lacquerware in Japan. Wajima's specific contribution is an undercoat technique that incorporates finely powdered diatomaceous earth (jinoko) from local deposits; the powder gives the undercoat an unusual hardness that allows Wajima pieces to survive daily use for generations. A Wajima soup bowl inherited from a grandparent is a common feature of older Japanese households.

Kyō-shikki, produced in Kyoto, carries the courtly and ceremonial end of the tradition. Kyoto lacquer is less built for daily use and more for display — tea ceremony implements, shrine fittings, imperial gift objects. The technique overlaps with Wajima in most respects, but the Kyoto tradition places more emphasis on the decorative maki-e (sprinkled-picture) technique in which gold and silver powder are fixed into the lacquer surface to produce the distinctive dark-ground-with-gold-highlights aesthetic of the finest Japanese decorative objects.

The single most famous maki-e artist in the modern tradition is Shōzan Matsuda (1896–1986), a Living National Treasure whose work is held in the Tokyo National Museum and the Museum of Modern Art, Ishikawa Prefecture. His successor as the defining modern master is Kōdō Ōba (b. 1943), also a Living National Treasure and still working at the time of writing.

Kintsugi: the repair as philosophy

The most widely known single use of urushi in modern discourse is not in the original construction of a lacquered object but in its repair. Kintsugi — literally "golden joinery" — is the Muromachi-period technique of repairing broken ceramics (and occasionally other materials) by filling the breaks with urushi lacquer mixed with powdered gold, so that the repair is deliberately visible as a network of gold veins running through the ceramic body. The earliest documented use of kintsugi is traditionally dated to the late fifteenth century and attributed to the shogun Ashikaga Yoshimasa, who — so the story goes — sent a broken Chinese tea bowl to China for repair, received it back with ugly metal staples, and commissioned Japanese craftsmen to develop a more beautiful alternative. Whether the story is historically accurate is debated; the technique itself is certainly attested from the Muromachi period onwards.

Kintsugi has, over the past two decades, become one of the most widely cited Japanese aesthetic concepts in English-language popular writing — often framed as a philosophy of embracing imperfection, of making visible the damage rather than concealing it, of understanding objects as having histories. The framing is not wrong, but it is somewhat thinner than the tradition itself, which is rooted specifically in the wabi-cha tea aesthetic and in a very particular Japanese attitude toward the value of objects that have survived and been repaired.

The global diffusion

Japanese lacquer reached Europe in quantity during the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries through the Dutch East India Company (VOC) trade, which operated from the artificial island of Dejima in Nagasaki harbour after Japan's 1639 closure to other foreign contact. The Dutch exported Japanese lacquered chests, cabinets, writing boxes, and tea caddies to Amsterdam, where they were distributed across European courts and became the most fashionable luxury objects of the period. Marie Antoinette owned a celebrated collection of fifty Japanese lacquer pieces, now in the Louvre. Louis XIV commissioned Japanese lacquer panels for the Trianon. Augustus the Strong of Saxony assembled the largest European lacquer collection of the period, now in the Porzellansammlung in Dresden.

The European demand was so great that it produced an entire category of imitation — what the English eventually called japanning: a European attempt to simulate Japanese lacquer using shellac, bitumen, and varnish on wood. The word entered English in the 1680s and gave us, eventually, the verb "to japan" (meaning to apply a hard black finish to metal or wood), and the adjective "japanned" (describing any surface so treated). The technique never quite matched true urushi — the chemistry is different, the durability is lower, and the depth of the finish never reaches the same register — but the European obsession with Japanese lacquer shaped the decorative arts of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries as thoroughly as the Chinese obsession with porcelain shaped the same period further south.

The sum is this: a tree native to East Asia, tapped by hand, produces a sap so distinctive in its cured form that Europe spent two centuries trying to imitate it and eventually borrowed the name of the country where it was perfected to describe the entire category of dark hard lacquered surfaces. Urushi is, in a literal etymological sense, Japan.

We find beauty not in the thing itself but in the patterns of shadows, the light and the darkness, that one thing against another creates. Attributed to Tanizaki Jun'ichirō, In Praise of Shadows (1933). Paraphrase of a central passage on Japanese lacquerware.
