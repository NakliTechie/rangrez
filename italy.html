Preface

Italy has, in some sense, the longest continuous chromatic tradition in the western Mediterranean. The painted plaster walls of Etruscan tombs at Tarquinia and Cerveteri, dating from the seventh century before the common era, already display a confident vocabulary of red, ochre, white, and black; the Roman fresco programmes of Pompeii and Herculaneum, sealed by the eruption of Vesuvius in 79 CE, preserve the most complete surviving record of any pre-modern domestic colour tradition anywhere in the world; and the Italian Renaissance painting of the fourteenth, fifteenth, and sixteenth centuries — from Giotto and Duccio through Piero della Francesca and Botticelli to Titian and Veronese — produces, on Italian panel and Italian wall, what is by general agreement the single most consequential body of work in the history of European art. The chromatic vocabulary that runs through all of this is remarkably continuous: the same red earths, the same yellow ochres, the same green earths, the same lime whites, applied in the same fresco technique on the same plastered walls, by craftsmen who were aware of their predecessors back to the Roman period and who consciously studied the surviving ancient examples whenever they could find them.

The single most important external addition to that continuous Italian chromatic vocabulary arrived from outside the peninsula and from outside Europe altogether. Lapis lazuli — the deep-blue silicate rock that the Persia chapter (§10.7 LAJVARD) traces upstream to a single mountain valley in Badakhshan, in what is now north-eastern Afghanistan — entered Italian painting through the Venetian trade with the eastern Mediterranean from the late twelfth century onwards. The technique for extracting the pure blue pigment from the raw rock was perfected in Italian workshops, was documented in detail by Cennino Cennini in his Il Libro dell'Arte around 1400, and produced over the following three centuries the specific saturated ultramarine that became, by contractual stipulation in painters' commissions, the colour of the Virgin's robe in essentially every major Italian altarpiece. Ultramarine was, weight for weight, more expensive than gold throughout the Renaissance; its application to a panel was a measurable index of the painting's value, and its presence or absence carried both theological and economic meaning. The ten palettes in this chapter walk through the Italian chromatic tradition from its Roman substrate through its medieval and Renaissance flowering to its modern expression, and the keystone slot at §13.7 is the lapis itself — the colour through which the Persia chapter and the China chapter and the Italy chapter all share a single material origin in the Hindu Kush.

Three role slots hold the anchors of the tradition: Carrara marble for LIGHT (the white block from which Michelangelo carved the Pietà and the David), parchment for WARM (the animal-skin substrate of medieval Italian manuscripts), and Florence's pietra serena for MONO (the blue-grey sandstone with which Brunelleschi set the dark interior trim of the early Renaissance churches against pale plaster walls). Six override slots carry Pompeian red, Tuscan sienna earth, the dark cypress of the Italian garden, the Iranian-Italian ultramarine of the keystone, the gold leaf of Byzantine and Trecento panel painting, and modern Italian espresso coffee — the only twentieth-century palette in the chapter and a deliberate acknowledgment that the Italian chromatic tradition has not stopped producing exports. The closing dark slot is the night of Venice, the city whose chromatic identity is the most complete and the most studied of any European city after Rome itself.

A Chinese chronology

  • c. 700 BCEThe Etruscan civilisation reaches its mature phase across central Italy. Tomb paintings at Tarquinia and Cerveteri produce the earliest surviving Italian wall-painting tradition, in red, ochre, white, and black on plaster.
  • 753 BCETraditional founding date of Rome. The Roman Republic will absorb the Etruscan tradition and continue the wall-painting and architectural-colour vocabulary essentially unchanged into the Imperial period.
  • 27 BCEOctavian becomes Augustus; the Roman Empire begins. Imperial Rome will host the largest single concentration of Mediterranean material culture in the ancient world.
  • 79 CEOn 24 August (the traditional date; some recent scholarship favours an October eruption), Vesuvius erupts and buries Pompeii, Herculaneum, Stabiae, and Oplontis. The frescoed walls of the buried towns are preserved almost intact for seventeen centuries before excavation begins.
  • c. 200 CERoman quarrying at Carrara intensifies. The white marble of the Apuan Alps becomes the standard imperial sculpture material and is shipped throughout the empire.
  • 313 CEThe Edict of Milan ends the Roman persecution of Christians. The first Christian basilicas are built in Rome over the following century, with mosaic decoration in Byzantine technique.
  • 476 CEThe Western Roman Empire collapses. The peninsula enters the long period of Byzantine, Lombard, Frankish, and city-state political fragmentation.
  • c. 1000 CEThe Italian maritime republics — Venice, Genoa, Pisa, Amalfi — begin systematic trade with the eastern Mediterranean. Venetian galleys carry, among many other goods, raw lapis lazuli from Badakhshan via Levantine intermediaries.
  • 1278 CECimabue's Maestà for Santa Trinita in Florence; one of the earliest large-scale Italian panel paintings to use Badakhshan ultramarine in quantity. Cimabue's pupil Giotto will carry the technique into the next generation.
  • 1308–1311Duccio di Buoninsegna paints the Maestà for Siena Cathedral. The work is the high point of the Sienese school and one of the most lavish surviving uses of ultramarine and gold leaf in any Italian altarpiece.
  • c. 1400 CECennino Cennini writes Il Libro dell'Arte in Padua. The treatise documents every working technique of the late-trecento Florentine workshop tradition, including the most detailed surviving mediaeval European recipe for extracting ultramarine pigment from raw lapis.
  • 1418 CEFilippo Brunelleschi wins the competition for the dome of Florence Cathedral. The construction (1420–1436) inaugurates the architectural Renaissance and establishes the Florentine pietra serena interior aesthetic.
  • 1508–1512Michelangelo paints the Sistine Chapel ceiling for Pope Julius II. The pigments include substantial Badakhshan ultramarine in the sky and in several of the prophets' robes.
  • 1516–1518Titian paints the Assumption of the Virgin for the high altar of the Frari in Venice. The Madonna's robe is ultramarine; the colour is among the defining Venetian uses of the pigment.
  • 1748 CESystematic archaeological excavation of Pompeii begins under the patronage of Charles III of Spain (later Charles VII of Naples). The Pompeian red of the buried frescoes enters the European decorative imagination within a generation.
  • 1828 CEJean-Baptiste Guimet wins the four-thousand-franc prize offered by the Société d'Encouragement pour l'Industrie Nationale for the synthesis of artificial ultramarine. Within twenty years, synthetic ultramarine has displaced natural lapis in most painters' studios; the Badakhshan trade collapses.
  • 1933 CEAlfonso Bialetti patents the Moka Express stovetop coffee maker in Crusinallo, Piedmont. The aluminium octagonal pot will become the single most ubiquitous Italian domestic object of the twentieth century.
  • 1948 CEAchille Gaggia patents the lever-piston espresso machine in Milan. The high-pressure extraction technique and the resulting crema on the surface of the cup define the modern Italian espresso aesthetic.
  • 1979 CEThe Archaeological Areas of Pompeii, Herculaneum and Torre Annunziata are inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage list. The Historic Centre of Florence follows in 1982; Venice and its Lagoon in 1987.
13.1

CarraraCarrara · White Marble§

Italian, after the Tuscan town of Carrara. The role slot for LIGHT.

The light role of the collection is the specific cool, faintly bluish white of Carrara marble — the dense crystalline limestone quarried from the Apuan Alps in northern Tuscany since the Roman period and the single most consequential sculpture stone in the history of Western art. Carrara is the white from which Michelangelo carved the David, the Pietà, and the Moses; it is the white of the columns of the Pantheon's interior; it is the white that, more than any other material, gives the European sculptural tradition its specific chromatic identity.

Material

Carrara marble is a metamorphic limestone composed almost entirely of recrystallised calcite, formed by the regional metamorphism of Mesozoic limestone deposits in the western Apuan Alps. The specific properties that have made it valuable for two and a half millennia are its exceptional purity (the calcite content is typically above 98 per cent, with very low iron and silica impurities), its fine and uniform grain (which allows the carver to take a clean edge in any direction), its translucency (a thin cut of Carrara transmits light to a depth of several millimetres, which is what gives finished marble sculpture its characteristic luminous surface), and its workability with iron and steel chisels. The colour is not pure white but a faintly bluish or faintly grey-warm white depending on the specific quarry and depth, and the best blocks — the so-called statuario grade — are reserved by the quarry operators for sculpture commissions and command prices several times the standard architectural-grade material.

The Apuan Alps quarries have been worked continuously since at least the second century BCE. The Romans developed the systematic extraction techniques — wedging, sawing with iron blades and abrasive sand, lowering blocks down the mountain on greased timber tracks — that are still recognisable in the modern operation. Imperial Roman Carrara was shipped throughout the empire and used in the Pantheon, the Trajan and Marcus Aurelius columns, and a substantial fraction of the surviving classical statuary in any major museum. The quarries went dormant after the fall of the Western Empire and were reopened in the early medieval period; by the time Michelangelo arrived in Carrara in 1497 to select blocks for his Pietà commission, the quarrying tradition was once again in continuous operation and remained so through the Renaissance, the Baroque, the Neoclassical period, and the modern industrial era.

Michelangelo at Carrara

Michelangelo Buonarroti (1475–1564) was the single most important consumer of Carrara marble in any individual generation. From his first major commission — the Pietà for Cardinal Jean de Bilhères, completed in Saint Peter's Basilica in 1499 — to his last unfinished sculptures, Michelangelo worked almost exclusively in Carrara stone, and he travelled personally to the Apuan Alps multiple times to select specific blocks for specific commissions. His correspondence from these visits, preserved in the Buonarroti family archive in Florence, contains some of the most detailed surviving accounts of how a Renaissance sculptor judged a piece of marble before purchase: the colour, the grain, the flaws, the position in the quarry face, the difficulty of extraction, and the price. The David (1501–1504), the Sistine Pietà, the Moses for the tomb of Pope Julius II, the four Slaves in the Accademia in Florence — all of them are Carrara, and most of them are from blocks Michelangelo selected himself.

The Renaissance Italian art-historical tradition treats the relationship between Michelangelo and Carrara marble as a paradigmatic example of the sculptor's discovery of the figure already latent within the stone. This is partly Michelangelo's own framing — his sonnets describe the figure as imprisoned in the marble and the sculptor's task as one of liberation — and partly Vasari's later codification in the Lives of the Artists (1550, expanded 1568). The framing has been criticised by modern scholars as romantic and misleading, but it remains the dominant lay understanding of how Renaissance sculpture worked.

13.2

PergamenaPergamena · Parchment§

Italian, from Latin pergamena, “of Pergamum.” The role slot for WARM.

The warm anchor of the collection is the soft cream-ivory of treated animal skin — the parchment and finer vellum on which the entire western European literary, legal, religious, and scholarly tradition was recorded between the fifth and the fifteenth centuries of the common era, before paper became cheap enough to displace it. Italian parchment carries a specific warm undertone that is distinct from the cooler, slightly greyer parchments of the northern European scriptoria, and the difference is part of how a trained eye dates and locates a medieval manuscript at first sight.

Material

Parchment is animal skin — most commonly sheep, goat, or calf — that has been soaked in lime to remove hair and flesh, scraped clean, stretched on a frame, dried under tension, and finally smoothed with pumice. The result is not leather (which is tanned) but a different material entirely: a thin, flexible, durable, and slightly translucent writing surface whose fibres run in two directions and whose finished sides — the hair side (slightly darker, slightly textured) and the flesh side (slightly lighter, slightly smoother) — can be distinguished even in a finished manuscript. The finest grade, made from the unborn or newborn calf, is called vellum in English (from Latin vitulinum, “of a calf”) and is the substrate of the most luxurious medieval European books.

The technique reaches Italy through the eastern Mediterranean. According to Pliny the Elder, parchment was perfected at Pergamum (modern Bergama in western Turkey) in the second century BCE under King Eumenes II, in response to an Egyptian embargo on papyrus exports. The story is partly legendary — animal-skin writing surfaces existed in the eastern Mediterranean long before Pergamum — but the city's name attached itself to the material and survives in every European language: Italian pergamena, French parchemin, English parchment, German Pergament.

The Italian manuscript tradition

From the sixth century onwards, Italian monastic scriptoria — at Monte Cassino, Subiaco, Bobbio, and the great Benedictine houses — produced parchment manuscripts on the local model, and from the eleventh century the universities of Bologna, Padua, and (later) Florence developed an entire commercial book trade based on parchment. The university stationers (stationarii) maintained authorised exemplars of the standard scholastic texts and rented them out to students and copyists in numbered sections (peciae) for transcription. The system produced enormous quantities of standardised parchment manuscripts during the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries, and the surviving books are still the foundation of every major European medieval manuscript collection.

The chromatic life of Italian parchment is the soft cream-ivory of the prepared skin, against which the medieval scribe's iron-gall ink reads as a deep brown-black and the illuminator's pigments — vermilion red lead, malachite green, azurite blue, and (for the most luxurious commissions) ultramarine derived from the same Badakhshan lapis that the keystone palette of this chapter follows — read as saturated and luminous. The interaction between parchment ground and applied colour is the basis of the entire European illuminated manuscript tradition, and the specific cream tone of Italian parchment — slightly warmer than its northern European counterparts because of differences in the local sheep and calf populations and the lime used in the preparation — is one of the visual signatures of the Italian medieval book.

13.3

Pietra SerenaPietra Serena · Florentine Sandstone§

Italian: “serene stone.” The role slot for MONO.

The monochrome role of the collection is the specific blue-grey-green sandstone quarried in the hills around Florence — the stone that Filippo Brunelleschi used to set the dark interior trim of his early-Renaissance churches against pale plaster walls and that, in his hands and the hands of his fifteenth-century followers, became the defining chromatic signature of the Florentine architectural interior. Pietra serena is not strictly grey and not strictly blue and not strictly green; it is a specific cool slate tone that resolves into different undertones depending on the light, and the entire interior aesthetic of the early Florentine Renaissance rests on its quiet authority.

Material

Pietra serena is a fine-grained Oligocene-period sandstone quarried in the Apennine foothills above Florence — principally at Fiesole, Maiano, and Settignano in the immediate hills, and historically also from quarries further north in the Mugello region. The chemical composition is mostly silica (quartz grains) bound in a matrix of clay and calcite, and the colour comes from a small content of glauconite, the iron-aluminium silicate that gives the stone its characteristic blue-grey-green cast. The grain is fine enough that the stone can be carved to a clean architectural profile — for capitals, bases, mouldings, door and window frames — and the colour is uniform enough across blocks that a long colonnade or a sequence of pilasters reads as a single chromatic element rather than as a collection of different stones.

The stone's working life on a building is roughly four centuries; thereafter it begins to weather visibly, particularly in exterior locations where rain and frost gradually break down the clay-calcite binder. The Florentine convention from the fifteenth century onwards has been to use pietra serena for interior architectural trim where the weathering is minimal and the colour stays stable, and to reserve the more durable Tuscan limestones for exterior structural work. The convention has held for six hundred years, and most of the Brunelleschi-period pietra serena interior work is still substantially intact.

Brunelleschi's chromatic invention

The single most consequential thing that happened to pietra serena in its history was Filippo Brunelleschi's decision, in the early 1420s, to use it as a structural-and-decorative element in the interiors of his new Florentine churches. Brunelleschi (1377–1446) had trained as a goldsmith and had visited Rome in his youth to study the surviving classical buildings; the chromatic vocabulary he developed on his return to Florence — pale plaster walls overlaid with a dark stone trim that articulated the structural lines of the building — was his solution to the problem of how to translate the Roman architectural orders into a form usable in a fifteenth-century Christian church.

The first complete realisation of the system is the Old Sacristy of San Lorenzo (1421–1428), commissioned by Giovanni di Bicci de' Medici. The room is a perfect cube topped by a hemispherical dome; the walls are pale plaster; and every architectural element — the pilasters, the entablature, the arches over the niches, the roundels of the dome — is articulated in pietra serena. The effect is one of mathematical clarity made visible: the structure of the building is legible in its colour, and the eye can read the proportions of the room as easily as a draughtsman can read a plan. Brunelleschi went on to use the same vocabulary in the nave of San Lorenzo, in Santo Spirito, and in the Pazzi Chapel at Santa Croce, and the formula was adopted by Michelozzo, by Giuliano da Sangallo, and by essentially every Florentine architect of the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries who built within the city walls. The interior chromatic identity of Florentine Renaissance architecture is, almost in its entirety, a Brunelleschi invention executed in pietra serena.

PompeianoPompeiano · Pompeian Red§

Italian, after the Roman city of Pompeii, buried by Vesuvius in 79 CE.

Pompeian red is the specific deep, slightly orange-shifted, slightly earthy red that runs across the wall frescoes of Pompeii, Herculaneum, Stabiae, and Oplontis — the four Roman towns sealed under volcanic ash by the eruption of Vesuvius in August 79 CE and gradually re-excavated from the eighteenth century onwards. The colour is one of the most chromatically distinctive surviving objects of the Roman material world; it has been imitated, reinterpreted, and quoted in European decorative painting since the first generation of Pompeian discoveries, and it has its own complications — not least the recent realisation that some of the famous Pompeian “red” walls were originally yellow ochre, and that the red we now see was created not by the original Roman painter but by the chemistry of the eruption itself.

Material

Roman wall painting was executed in buon fresco — pigment applied to wet lime plaster, where the lime carbonates as it dries and locks the pigment chemically into the wall surface. The technique produces an extraordinarily durable colour layer that can survive for two thousand years if the wall remains intact. Pompeian red, in its original Roman form, was produced from one of two mineral pigments: cinnabar (mercury sulfide, the same vermilion that the China chapter discusses at §2.8 ZHUSHA), or red ochre (an iron-oxide earth, more common and considerably cheaper). The cinnabar walls were the luxury option and were reserved for the most expensive private houses; the red ochre walls were the standard everyday choice and account for the majority of surviving examples.

The chromatic effect of a Pompeian red wall is unmistakable. The red is deep and saturated but not glossy; the lime substrate gives the surface a slight chalkiness that softens the edges of any painted decoration; and the colour's interaction with the figurative scenes painted over it — the still lifes, the mythological tableaux, the architectural perspectives — is one of the defining visual experiences of any visit to the Pompeii archaeological site. The Villa of the Mysteries (Villa dei Misteri), with its great frieze of Dionysian initiation against a continuous Pompeian-red ground, is the canonical example.

The 2011 finding

In 2011, a team of researchers led by Sergio Augusti and other Italian and Spanish conservators published findings on a series of Herculaneum and Pompeii walls demonstrating that some of what had been catalogued for two centuries as Pompeian red was, in fact, originally yellow ochre. The transformation appears to have happened during the eruption itself: the high temperatures of the pyroclastic flow, combined with the presence of volcanic gases (specifically reducing conditions and sulfurous compounds), converted the yellow iron-oxide pigment into a red iron-oxide form. The change is chemical and irreversible, and it means that some of the most famous “red” walls of Pompeii — including significant portions of the wall paintings in the House of the Vettii — were never red while their Roman owners were alive.

The finding does not undermine the existence of Pompeian red as a Roman colour; many of the surviving red walls were genuinely red from the moment of their original execution. But it complicates the iconography in a specific and interesting way: the popular nineteenth- and twentieth-century European reception of Pompeian red as the canonical Roman wall colour, and the subsequent quotation of the colour in European Neoclassical interior decoration from Robert Adam in 1760s England through to twentieth-century revival movements, was based on a partial misreading of the archaeological evidence. The “Pompeian red” of the European decorative tradition is a cultural construct as much as a historical fact.

SienaSiena · Burnt & Raw Sienna§

Italian, after the Tuscan city of Siena. The standard earth-pigment family.

Siena is a family of iron-oxide earth pigments — both the unprocessed yellow-brown raw sienna and the kiln-roasted reddish-brown burnt sienna — that have been quarried from the hills around the Tuscan city of Siena since the Middle Ages and that have been a standard component of every European painter's palette for at least seven hundred years. The colour is unobtrusive but indispensable: virtually no Italian Renaissance panel painting is without some sienna in its underlayers, and virtually no twentieth-century European art-school student has graduated without owning a tube of each.

Material

Sienna pigments are iron-oxide-rich clay earths in which the colorant is a hydrated iron oxide called limonite or goethite. Raw sienna is the natural unfired earth, which produces a warm yellow-brown colour with a slightly greenish undertone. Burnt sienna is the same earth after roasting in a kiln at moderate temperatures — typically around 250–400°C — which dehydrates the pigment, converts the limonite into haematite (an anhydrous iron oxide), and shifts the colour from yellow-brown into a deep, slightly orange-shifted red-brown. The transition happens gradually as the temperature rises, and skilled pigment makers historically produced a range of intermediate shades by varying the firing time and temperature.

The pigment is named after the city of Siena, in whose surrounding hills the most famous historical deposits were located. The Sienese pigment trade is documented from at least the early Middle Ages and reached its commercial peak during the Renaissance, when sienna was exported throughout the Italian peninsula and into northern Europe through the Venetian trade. The original Sienese deposits are now largely exhausted, and modern commercial sienna pigment is sourced primarily from Sicily, Sardinia, and Cyprus, but the name has stuck: every artist's tube of “raw sienna” or “burnt sienna” still carries the name of the Tuscan city, even when the actual material was dug from a Mediterranean island five hundred miles away.

Use in the painter's palette

Sienna's specific value to a painter is twofold. First, the colour itself: the warm reddish-brown of burnt sienna fills a slot in the palette that no other pigment occupies as cleanly, and the related raw sienna provides a cooler companion in the same family. Second, the working properties: sienna is exceptionally lightfast, mixes well with virtually all other pigments without producing chemical instabilities, dries at a moderate rate in oil, and is sufficiently inexpensive to use in quantity for underpaintings and ground layers. The combination is the reason sienna has survived as a studio standard from the late mediaeval period through the present day, while many fashionable pigments of the same era have come and gone.

Cennino Cennini, in Il Libro dell'Arte around 1400 (the same treatise that documents the ultramarine extraction recipe in §13.7), describes a “natural earth which can be found in the country of Siena” that is essentially raw sienna and gives instructions for its use in panel painting. Pliny the Elder, in his Natural History, mentions Italian iron-oxide earths under the name sinopia — strictly speaking sinopia was the red earth from the Black Sea port of Sinope, but the Italian usage extended the name to similar iron-oxide earths from the Italian peninsula, including those from Siena. The terminology has remained somewhat unstable across the medieval and Renaissance periods, and individual sources do not always distinguish carefully between Sienese earth, sinopia, and various other iron-oxide pigments.

13.6

CipressoCipresso · Cypress§

Italian: Mediterranean cypress, Cupressus sempervirens.

Cipresso is the specific deep blue-green-grey of the Mediterranean cypress — the columnar evergreen that lines the driveways of Tuscan villas, marks the boundaries of cemeteries across the Italian peninsula, and provides the vertical accent of every classical Italian garden from the Boboli in Florence to the Villa d'Este at Tivoli. The colour is dark, slightly dusty, slightly bluish-green, and exceptionally stable across seasons; cypress trees do not change colour with the year, and a row of them seen from a distance is one of the most recognisable visual signatures of the Italian landscape.

Material

Cupressus sempervirens is a slow-growing evergreen conifer native to the eastern Mediterranean and naturalised across the western Mediterranean basin, where it has been cultivated for landscape and timber use since the Roman period and probably earlier. The tree's natural form is mildly conical, but the columnar variety that dominates the Italian landscape — Cupressus sempervirens var. stricta — is a cultivar selected over centuries for its vertical, narrow, almost flame-like silhouette. Mature trees commonly reach twenty to thirty metres in height with a crown only two or three metres wide, which gives them the distinctive vertical-line quality on which the entire Italian garden tradition relies.

The chromatic value of cypress in the landscape comes from two interlocking properties. First, the foliage colour: the small scale-like leaves are a deep dusty blue-green that does not change with the season, so a row of cypresses provides a constant chromatic anchor against which the seasonal changes of olive groves, deciduous oaks, and field crops can register. Second, the vertical line: the narrow columnar profile reads as a structural element rather than a botanical one, and a row of cypresses functions in a Tuscan landscape composition the way a colonnade functions in a classical building.

The Italian garden

The Italian Renaissance garden — formalised in the sixteenth century and codified in the seventeenth — uses cypress as one of its three principal vegetal elements, alongside box hedging and stone pine. The classical model is the Villa d'Este at Tivoli (built 1550–1572 for Cardinal Ippolito II d'Este), where rows of cypresses frame the central axial perspectives and provide the vertical contrast against which the elaborate water features read. The Boboli Gardens behind the Pitti Palace in Florence (laid out from 1549 onwards for Cosimo I de' Medici), the Villa Lante at Bagnaia (1566), and the Villa Aldobrandini at Frascati (early seventeenth century) all use the same vocabulary, and the formula spread across Europe over the following two centuries — the cypress alley remains a recognisable element of formal garden design from Versailles to the English landscape garden tradition.

The cultural weight of the cypress in Italian visual tradition is reinforced by its long association with mortality. Cypresses are planted in Italian cemeteries by ancient convention — a practice that goes back at least to the Roman period and that may carry an Etruscan precedent — and the dark vertical line of a cypress against a Mediterranean sky carries, for many Italian readers, an immediate funerary association. The connection is not absolute (the cypress avenues of the Boboli are not funerary), but it sits in the chromatic background of every cypress slot in any Italian visual composition.

LapislazzuliLapislazzuli · Renaissance Ultramarine§

Italian: lapis lazuli; “the stone from across the sea.” The keystone of the chapter.

Lapislazzuli is the keystone of the Italy chapter, and it is the third successive chapter in the Bibliothèque whose keystone is the same blue. The Persia chapter's §10.7 LAJVARD followed the Badakhshan lapis upstream to the Sar-i Sang mines in north-eastern Afghanistan; the China chapter's §2.7 QINGHUA followed it across the Mongol-period trade network into the Jingdezhen porcelain kilns; and this chapter follows it westward through the Venetian trade into the workshops of fourteenth-century Florence and Padua, where Cennino Cennini documented the extraction recipe around 1400 and where the resulting pigment — ultramarine, “from beyond the sea” — became, weight for weight, more expensive than gold and the contractually specified colour of the Virgin's robe in essentially every major Italian altarpiece for the next three centuries.

Material & chemistry

Lapis lazuli is a metamorphic rock composed principally of the mineral lazurite (a sodium-calcium aluminosilicate carrying sulfide ions, which produce the characteristic deep-blue colour), with smaller quantities of sodalite, hauynite, and calcite, and tiny inclusions of pyrite (iron sulfide) that appear as the golden flecks scattered through the finest specimens. The lazurite content is what determines the chromatic intensity of the finished pigment: the best Badakhshan rock contains between twenty-five and forty per cent lazurite, with very little calcite to whiten the tone. Lower-grade lapis from Chile or Lake Baikal — both of which were exploited only from the nineteenth century onwards — shows considerably more calcite and produces a greyer, less saturated blue.

Extracting the pigment from the raw rock is the technical challenge that made Italian ultramarine so expensive. Simply grinding the rock produces a dirty grey-blue powder in which the lazurite is mixed with all the inert minerals from the matrix; to produce the saturated pigment that the Italian painters wanted, the lazurite has to be separated from the matrix by a process of differential extraction. Cennino Cennini, in Il Libro dell'Arte, gives the most detailed surviving mediaeval European description of the technique. The ground rock is mixed with a paste of pine resin, mastic, beeswax, and linseed oil, kneaded into a single workable mass, and then washed repeatedly in a weak lye solution; the lazurite particles separate from the matrix and migrate into the wash water, from which they are recovered as the finished pigment. The first wash yields the purest and most intensely coloured grade; subsequent washes yield progressively paler grades; and the final residue, after all the lazurite has been extracted, is discarded. A single working batch can take several days, requires substantial quantities of expensive resin and oil, and is the kind of operation that a fifteenth-century painter typically did not perform himself but commissioned from a specialist pigment supplier.

The yield is the reason for the price. One kilogram of finished top-grade ultramarine pigment commonly required five or more kilograms of raw lapis, several days of skilled labour, and significant quantities of consumable materials. The cost per gram of the finished pigment, throughout the Italian Renaissance, was higher than the cost per gram of gold leaf — and unlike gold leaf, which is a structural decoration that can be applied across an entire panel ground at relatively modest material cost, ultramarine had to be used as a paint, mixed with binder, and applied stroke by stroke to a finished design. The economics meant that the painter's contractual obligations to a patron commonly specified the exact weight of ultramarine to be used, the locations on the panel where it was to be applied, and the price adjustments to be made if the agreed quantity was reduced.

The Venetian trade route

Lapis lazuli reached Italy through the Venetian commercial network from at least the late twelfth century onwards. The Venetian galleys that traded into the eastern Mediterranean — to Constantinople, to Acre, to Alexandria, and to the Crusader ports of the Levant — encountered Persian and Arab merchants who carried lapis westward from the Hindu Kush along the same overland routes that brought silk, spices, and precious stones from central Asia. The rough rock was traded in lump form in the Venetian markets and then sold on to specialist pigment makers, principally in Venice itself, in Florence, in Padua, and in Naples. The Italian word oltremarino (“from beyond the sea”) and its English derivative ultramarine both refer to the long voyage of the raw material from Afghanistan to the Italian peninsula; the colour was named for its origin rather than for its hue.

The Venetian role is the connecting link between the Persia chapter and the Italy chapter. The same trade network that the Persia chapter follows outward from the Sar-i Sang mines also reached, by water rather than by land, the Adriatic coast of Italy. Venice was the western terminus of one of the great pre-modern lapis trade routes, and the Florentine and Paduan pigment industries were the downstream consumers of the Venetian import trade. From the Italian workshops, the finished ultramarine pigment was then re-exported across the rest of Europe — to the manuscript scriptoria of France and Germany, to the Flemish panel painters, and (more slowly) to the Spanish, Portuguese, and English markets. By the seventeenth century, when Vermeer in Delft was rationing his ultramarine in compositions like Girl with a Pearl Earring (c. 1665), the pigment in his studio had reached him through a supply chain that began at the same Afghan mountain valley that supplied Tutankhamun's funerary mask and the qinghua porcelain of the Jingdezhen kilns.

Cennini and the Italian recipe

Cennino d'Andrea Cennini was a Florentine painter active in Padua at the end of the fourteenth century — a pupil of Agnolo Gaddi, who was himself a pupil of Taddeo Gaddi, who was a pupil of Giotto. His Il Libro dell'Arte, written around 1400, is the single most important surviving technical manual of the late-trecento Italian workshop tradition: it covers drawing, panel preparation, fresco, egg tempera, gilding, the preparation of pigments, the recipes for binders, and the practical economics of the studio. Chapter sixty-two of the treatise gives the full ultramarine extraction recipe in step-by-step detail, and the extant English translation by Daniel V. Thompson, Jr. (first published in 1933 and reprinted by Dover in 1954) is the standard scholarly edition. Cennini's praise for the finished colour is famous in the literature.

A colour illustrious, beautiful, and most perfect, beyond all other colours. Cennino Cennini, Il Libro dell'Arte, chapter LXII, on ultramarine. c. 1400. Public domain in the Daniel V. Thompson translation.

The contractual specification of ultramarine

The economics of ultramarine produced a specific contractual practice in Italian Renaissance painting. A patron commissioning an altarpiece or a private devotional panel would frequently specify, in the written contract with the painter, the exact quality and quantity of ultramarine to be used. The contracts that survive — many of them transcribed in Michael Baxandall's classic 1972 study Painting and Experience in Fifteenth Century Italy — are remarkably specific. A typical clause might require the painter to use ultramarine at a price of four florins per ounce (the highest grade), to apply it to the Madonna's robe to a specified standard of saturation, and to forfeit a portion of his fee if the agreed quantity was not delivered. The patron's interest was in part theological (the Virgin deserves the most expensive pigment) and in part economic (a contract specifying ultramarine produced a more valuable finished object that could later be sold or inherited at a higher price).

The practice declined gradually over the course of the sixteenth century as the chromatic vocabulary of Italian painting expanded, as patrons became more interested in the painter's skill than in the value of his materials, and as cheaper substitutes for ultramarine — principally azurite, a copper carbonate that produces a slightly greener and considerably less stable blue — became more widely accepted. By the seventeenth century the contractual specification of pigments had largely disappeared, though the economic distinction between ultramarine and other blues persisted in the studio practice of individual painters. Vermeer's reputation for using more ultramarine than his contemporary Dutch colleagues, even when he was struggling financially in the 1670s, is one of the better-known anecdotes about the late history of the pigment in European studio practice.

The collapse of the natural lapis trade

Natural ultramarine remained the standard saturated blue of European painting until the late 1820s, when the French chemist Jean-Baptiste Guimet succeeded in synthesising the pigment artificially. Guimet's process, which used kaolin, sodium carbonate, sulphur, and charcoal heated together in a closed crucible, produced a chemically identical lazurite-based pigment at a fraction of the cost of the natural material. The Société d'Encouragement pour l'Industrie Nationale had offered a prize of four thousand francs for the synthesis in 1824; Guimet claimed it in 1828 (a German chemist, Christian Gmelin, independently developed the same process at roughly the same time, but Guimet got the prize). Within twenty years, synthetic ultramarine — known in the trade as “French ultramarine” — had displaced the natural material in most painters' studios, the Badakhshan trade had collapsed, and the centuries-long monopoly of the Hindu Kush mines on the European blue pigment market was over.

The chromatic effect of the change is subtle but real. Synthetic ultramarine is, on average, slightly more uniform in colour than the natural material and slightly less prone to the small variations in undertone that distinguish one batch of natural lapis from another. Conservation analysis can usually distinguish the two — the synthetic material has a different particle morphology under the microscope and a different infrared spectrum — and the distinction is essential for dating and authenticating European paintings of the early-to-mid nineteenth century, where the appearance of synthetic ultramarine in a work that purports to be from the 1810s or earlier is a strong indicator of later restoration or outright forgery.

OroOro · Gold Leaf§

Italian: gold. Specifically gold leaf as ground and decoration in panel painting.

Oro is gold leaf — beaten gold hammered to a thickness of less than a tenth of a micrometre and applied as a ground or as a decorative element to wood panels, manuscript pages, and frescoed walls. In the Italian medieval and early Renaissance painting tradition, gold leaf is not merely a decoration but a structural element of the composition: a Trecento Sienese altarpiece or a Florentine devotional panel from the same period commonly carries a continuous gold ground behind the figures, and the chromatic effect of that ground — light-catching, slightly warm, slightly textured — is one of the defining visual experiences of Italian Trecento painting.

Material & technique

Gold leaf is produced by beating thin sheets of gold between layers of parchment or vellum (the gold-beater's skins) until the metal has been reduced to a leaf less than a tenth of a micrometre thick. The process is slow, requires considerable skill, and produces leaf so thin that it is essentially translucent in transmitted light and weighs almost nothing. The Italian gold-beating trade was historically concentrated in Florence and Venice, and the surviving technical literature — Cennini covers it in Il Libro dell'Arte, chapters one hundred to one hundred and twenty — describes both the production and the application of the leaf in considerable detail.

The application of gold leaf to a panel painting follows a specific technical sequence. The wood support is prepared with several layers of gesso (gypsum mixed with rabbit-skin glue), sanded smooth, and then coated with a layer of bole — a fine red-brown clay, traditionally Armenian bole, that provides both an adhesion layer for the gold and a warm undertone that shows through any gaps in the leaf. The leaf is then applied wet, pressed gently into the bole with a soft brush, and allowed to dry. Once dry, the gold can be burnished with a smooth stone (typically agate) to produce a high-polish mirror surface, or left matte for a softer effect. The skilled gilder can produce both finishes on the same panel and use the contrast as a compositional element.

The Sienese and Florentine schools

The two great centres of Italian gold-ground panel painting in the Trecento were Siena and Florence, and the two cities developed slightly different approaches to the same basic vocabulary. The Sienese school, founded by Duccio di Buoninsegna (c. 1255–1319) and continued by Simone Martini, the Lorenzetti brothers, and a long line of fourteenth-century followers, used gold ground in a relatively flat and decorative way: the figures are placed against a continuous gold field that reads as a non-spatial sacred environment rather than as a depicted location. The Florentine school, founded by Giotto di Bondone (c. 1267–1337) and continued by Taddeo Gaddi, Bernardo Daddi, and Maso di Banco, gradually moved away from the flat gold ground toward depicted spatial settings — landscapes, architectural interiors, sky — even while continuing to use gold for haloes, decorative borders, and individual ornamental elements.

The transition from gold-ground to depicted-space painting is one of the central narratives of early Renaissance art history, and it is essentially the story of how Florentine painting moved from the Trecento to the Quattrocento between the 1330s and the 1430s. By the time Masaccio painted the Brancacci Chapel (1424–1428) and the Trinity at Santa Maria Novella (1425), gold ground had largely been abandoned in serious Florentine practice in favour of atmospheric perspective; the gold survived in haloes, in liturgical objects, and in deliberate archaisms, but not as the primary chromatic field of a painting. The Sienese tradition, more conservative, continued to use gold ground well into the fifteenth century — Sassetta, Giovanni di Paolo, and Sano di Pietro all produce mid-Quattrocento Sienese altarpieces with substantial gold-ground passages — and the survival of the technique in Siena is part of what gives the city's fifteenth-century painting its distinctive backward-looking flavour.

EspressoEspresso · Italian Coffee§

Italian: pressed-out coffee. The modern Italian cultural export.

Espresso is the specific deep brown — almost black at the centre, with a hazel-coloured layer of crema on the surface — of properly extracted Italian coffee, produced by forcing pressurised hot water through a tightly packed bed of finely ground roasted coffee. It is the only twentieth-century palette in the Italy chapter, and its presence here is a deliberate acknowledgment that the Italian chromatic tradition has not stopped exporting; the modern espresso bar is, by some reasonable measures, the single most successful Italian aesthetic object of the post-war period.

Material & method

Espresso is defined by its method rather than by its bean. The coffee is roasted to a moderate-to-dark level (Italian commercial roasts run somewhat darker than the equivalent third-wave specialty roasts), ground to a specific consistency (finer than drip coffee but coarser than Turkish), packed into a portafilter at a specific weight (typically seven to nine grams for a single shot, fourteen to eighteen grams for a double), and extracted by forcing hot water at roughly nine bars of pressure through the coffee bed for approximately twenty-five to thirty seconds. The result is a small volume of intensely concentrated coffee — typically twenty-five to thirty millilitres for a single shot — topped by a layer of crema, the hazel-coloured emulsion of coffee oils, water, and dissolved carbon dioxide that forms on the surface during extraction and that is one of the visual signatures of properly made espresso.

The chromatic specifics of a good espresso are diagnostic. The body of the drink should be a deep mahogany brown, almost opaque but not quite black; the crema layer should be a warm hazel colour with a slightly variegated texture (sometimes called “tiger striping”) and should persist for at least a minute before breaking down; and the boundary between the body and the crema should be clearly visible in side profile. A barista watching a shot extract reads the colour and the flow rate moment by moment, and an experienced practitioner can identify a misadjusted grind, a wrong dose, or a stale bean from the colour of the stream alone.

The two patents

The modern Italian coffee aesthetic rests on two specific twentieth-century inventions, both Italian, both still in continuous production. The first is the Bialetti Moka Express, the octagonal aluminium stovetop coffee maker patented by Alfonso Bialetti in 1933 in the small Piedmontese town of Crusinallo. The Moka pot uses steam pressure (typically one to two bars, considerably less than a true espresso machine) to push hot water from a lower chamber through a bed of ground coffee into an upper chamber, producing a strong brewed coffee that, while not technically espresso, occupies the same chromatic and aesthetic register and is the standard domestic coffee of Italian households. The original octagonal aluminium design has remained essentially unchanged for ninety years, and the company estimates that more than three hundred million units have been sold.

The second is the Gaggia espresso machine, the lever-piston bar machine patented by Achille Gaggia in Milan in 1948. The Gaggia design replaced the earlier steam-only espresso machines (which produced espresso at low pressure and without crema) with a spring-loaded lever that the barista pulled by hand to force water through the coffee bed at nine bars of pressure. The high-pressure extraction was the technical innovation that produced crema for the first time, and the visual identity of modern espresso — the mahogany body topped by the hazel layer — dates specifically to Gaggia's machine. The Gaggia design was widely imitated by other Italian manufacturers (Faema, La Marzocco, La Pavoni) and became the basis of the worldwide commercial espresso industry.

The Italian coffee bar as cultural form

The cultural unit on which the Italian coffee tradition rests is not the cup but the bar — the small, standing-room-mostly establishment in which a customer orders, pays, drinks an espresso at the counter, and leaves, all within five or ten minutes. The Italian bar is open from early morning until late evening, serves coffee, water, simple food, and (later in the day) wine and spirits, and functions as a kind of communal living room for the surrounding neighbourhood. The price of an espresso at a typical Italian bar in 2025 is around one euro twenty centimes — kept low by long-standing custom and by the assumption that the drink is a daily necessity rather than a luxury — and the quality of the coffee is, by any international standard, extraordinarily high for the price. The Italian bar is one of the very few categories of consumer object in modern Europe in which the everyday standard exceeds the luxury standard in most other countries.

Notte VenezianaNotte Veneziana · Venice at Night§

Italian: Venetian night. The role slot for DARK.

The dark role of the collection is the specific deep blue-black of Venice at night — the colour of the lagoon under a moonless sky, of the Grand Canal seen from the water at midnight, of the unlit interiors of the Frari and the Scuola di San Rocco when the last of the day's light has gone. Venice is one of the very few European cities whose night-time chromatic identity has been the subject of substantial literary and visual attention in its own right, and the dark slot of the Italy chapter belongs to the city that has, more than any other, made its own darkness into a chromatic argument.

The chromatic identity of Venice

Venice is a small city — its historic core is roughly seven square kilometres, with a population of about fifty thousand permanent residents — built on a series of low islands in a shallow lagoon at the top of the Adriatic. It has no automobiles, no street lighting on the canal-fronts in the older quarters, and (in the absence of significant ambient light pollution from the surrounding mainland) a genuinely dark night sky over the central districts. The combination of water, low buildings, and the absence of cars means that a night-time walk through Venice produces a chromatic experience that almost no other major European city can match: the canals are a deep blue-black almost indistinguishable from the sky, the limestone facades of the palaces register as pale grey against the darker stone of the calli, and the few illuminated windows produce isolated points of warm yellow light that read as the only chromatic incident in an otherwise nearly monochrome composition.

The standard daylight chromatic identity of Venice is somewhat different: the warm pinks, ochres, and burnt oranges of the plastered house facades, the brick-red of the bell towers, the glittering gold mosaics of San Marco. But the night register is the more carefully attended one in the literary tradition. The English-speaking cultural imagination of Venice — from Byron and Ruskin in the early nineteenth century through Henry James, Thomas Mann, Marcel Proust, and Joseph Brodsky in the twentieth — is overwhelmingly nocturnal, and the city's most famous literary and musical evocations are night pieces.

The literature of Venetian night

The single most important English-language treatment of Venice as a chromatic and literary object is Jan Morris's Venice (Faber & Faber, 1960; revised 1974, 1993, 2014), the long meditative essay-portrait that the Anglo-Welsh writer (then James Morris) compiled during a year of residence in the city in the late 1950s. The book is written in the present tense, organised by theme rather than by neighbourhood, and treats Venice not as a tourist destination but as a working city with its own physical and emotional logic. The night chapters are among the strongest in the book, and Morris's description of the lagoon at midnight remains the canonical English-language statement of the dark register of the Venetian palette.

The other essential modern Venetian text is Joseph Brodsky's Watermark (Farrar, Straus & Giroux, 1992), a short prose meditation on the city written by the Russian-American Nobel laureate during his many winter visits in the 1970s and 1980s. Brodsky was unusual among the great twentieth-century literary visitors to Venice in that he came almost exclusively in winter, when the tourist traffic is at its minimum, the city is at its coldest and quietest, and the night-time chromatic register is at its most concentrated. Brodsky was eventually buried in the cemetery of San Michele in 1996, in the Protestant section, by his own request.

The visual tradition of Venice at night is somewhat narrower than the literary tradition. Canaletto and Guardi, the two great eighteenth-century Venetian view-painters, painted the city almost exclusively in daylight; the nocturnal Venice of Whistler's nocturnes (1879–1880) and of John Singer Sargent's later watercolours is largely a nineteenth-century English-American invention, and the modern photographic tradition of night-time Venice (visible in any reasonable city guidebook) is essentially a twentieth-century phenomenon enabled by long-exposure photography on portable cameras.
